The Subject: Re: 'Offense' and the Virgin Birth
At 08:33:01 on 01/22/97, Tom Dean (tsd@ogk.com) wrote:

SK's metaphysics, by treating the universal and the absolute as separate realms, seems to blind him to the process by which every absolute event becomes universal history: We all know Abraham's sacrifice (his experience of the absolute) only through the realm of the universal, as a document subject to redaction and doubt. The facticity of the story of Abraham (like that of Jesus Christ) matters to us, because without him we can know the existence of the absolute only by our own paltry experience. If Abraham and JC don't exist, we lack context within which to experience the absolute. If they aren't who we have been told -- if the reports aren't reliable, we may be impeded from entering a proper relation to the absolute.

The test of old stories is in the veracity of the witnesses to them, and in consideration of other evidence in the world to see if it supports the testimony (as one studies the lay of the land on the Waterloo battlefield today to see if the reports of troop movements from 1815 are believable). I think SK drifts away at times from recognizing that there is a necessary step through the realm of the universal on the way to the absolute. Ironically, the man whose lifelong annoyance was with those who immediately went beyond faith appears himself to have skipped a necessary preliminary step to faith.

That is, SK's metaphysics discounts the value of testing the messengers (an action with reference to the universal), and so he resorts to trying to test the Message (the absolute), but such a test is untimely. The Message comes to us now only through intermediaries we know in the realm of the universal -- dogma and books. The test of faith in the 20th century cannot be trying to approach JC as His contemporary could. It has to be testing the witnesses to the events, and seeking verification in the experiences we have. Brown, therefore, appears to me to be engaging in the step that SK skips. We have to test Matthew's veracity before we can fully achieve faith.
