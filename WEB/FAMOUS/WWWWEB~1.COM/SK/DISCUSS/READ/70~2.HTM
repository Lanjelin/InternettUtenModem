At 08:55:17 on 08/27/96, James Smith (sjames@tron.cochise.cc.az.us) wrote:

At 11:24:51 PST on 08-26-96, J. Climacus wrote:

> Well, sure: My child is brain-dead, in a vegetative state, and
>god says to take him out. I'll buy that (or at least that's a possible
>situation in which I might consider it). Or maybe god tells me my son
>is going to grow up to be the next Hitler. I might go along with that as
>well. But I think the point of the Abraham parable is that Abraham was
>expected to do it just because god sez so. Any reasons god might give
>to JUSTIFY his commands means he is in some way subservient to a code
>of ethics or morality, that he is not the source of what is right, but
>is constrained by convention or tradition or human morality to only do
>that which is acceptable to mere mortals. I'm perfectly happy to accept
>this conception of god (although I don't think such a god would be any
>more likely to exist). But that's not the god of Abraham or the god of
>orthodox christianity.

I think you're missing the point in a way, Johannes. In the end, Abraham was
not expected to kill his son; Abraham's hope that he wouldn't have to
kill Isaac and God's promise that Abraham would be the patriarch of a large
race both proved true. For God, for whom (I believe) there is no time, there
was no question that Abraham wouldn't kill Isaac. Abraham, though, had to face those
questions, had to deal with his doubts that any of God's promises would ever come
true. God wasn't asking Abraham to kill Isaac for just any reason--it was so that
Abraham (and now the rest of us) would trust God, no matter the hopelessness or absurdity
of the situation. In the end, Johannes, I still think you're taking the test
outside of the whole context of the Abraham story. Nonetheless, I'm going to try
to preliminarily answer the question you asked below: "What is faith?" down there, and hopefully
I can describe what I believe a little better.
