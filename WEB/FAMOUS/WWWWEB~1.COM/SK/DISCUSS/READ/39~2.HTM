The Subject: Re: Abraham
At 01:48:11 on 08/06/96, Anthony L. Pecoraro (alp@loop.com) wrote:

At 20:29:37 PST on 08-05-96, J. Climacus wrote:

> I think viewing Abraham as a model of correct behavior is to attempt to recast his actions in an ethical framework. I think the point of Abraham's dilemma is that the demands of faith are incommensurate with a life in, as you say, the polis. As a professor of mine once said, there is little to extract from the Abraham/Isaac story except maybe: It is a terrible to be in the presence of the Living God. For me, the story of Abraham, to the extent that it can be made into any kind of sense, is that the demands of God and faith will always be outside the matrix of conceptual thought. Ethical categories simply don't apply.

Abraham is to soothe the children aberrant in a singular
star system. Ethics is incommensurate only in terms of
the level of mutation of the species and the degree to
which the light of knowledge is prevalent as potentiality.

The "terrible" you (or dumb professor) speak of is never
being in the presence of God, but ignorance in the
presence of God, as in the ignorance of brute in spite
of his starry (godly) nature.

But then you are correct: The true nature of the biblical
teachings are not within the realm of thought; they can
only be a direct experiencing (pure experience) much
like the direct cognition of universal structure so
vacant in [our] western culture.

ALP, 8/6/96 (Book 36)
