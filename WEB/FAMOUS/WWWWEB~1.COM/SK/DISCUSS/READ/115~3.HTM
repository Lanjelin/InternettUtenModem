Hello.
Thanks so much for responding to my cry for help. I'm not sure I agree with
what your interpretations however. (Hey, but what do I know? I'm the one who
publicly announced that I don't "get it.")

The way I see it (and as I've been told) Kierkegaard claims there are
different classes of people: those in lower immediacy, the ethical, knights
of resignation and knights of faith. God is only a representation of your
duty towards... yeah towards what?

Well, here's that defining relationship thing again. What I mean by that is
the absolute commitment (to a finite being, thus VULNERABLE) that tells you
what to do everyday, rules your world. This is all in the Preamble.
Kierkegaard was committed to Regina (to whom this was all written in an
explanation as to why he broke off their engagement) Abraham was committed
to Issac, The prince to the princess and so on. It has to be something that
cannot be explained too (good thing 'cause I can't do it). Like SK couldn't
explain to Mr. X why he was absolutely committed to R. SK writes as a knight
of resignation so he has accepted the fact that he can live without R and
does. Abraham was a knight of faith so he lives with Issac realizing that he
is vulnerable (and that he could live without him) but still lives as if he
is the most sure thing in the world.

There is nothing great about what Abraham did. Not to me anyway. I see it as
a demonstration only of Abrahams Faith in Issac. Abraham experiences a
conflict between his ethical duty (duty to neighbors, world, christian) and
his "faith duty" toward Issac. The ethical duty is not to kill Isaac. Makes
sense. Somehow Abraham's faith duty was to kill him. (Don't ask...I have no
answer.) The reason I say Abraham was sure he would get Issac back is
because he was a knight of faith "living as if Issac was invulnerable."
Paradoxical Faith in the ABSURD.

So, I think God is only a representation of your duty toward the object of
your absolute commitment.

RE: RE: RE: EXERCISE IN FUTILITY

I don't think Kierkegaard wants us to do anything. I think he's pointing out
how he sees it. Ask the question "Is there a teleological suspension of the
ethical?" Get an answer. Lower immediacy: OF COURSE they'll do anything to
serve themselves. Ethical:NO. unless it is for the higher ethical (steal
medicine to save life) Knight of Faith:YES. They can suspend the ethical for
the sake of their commitment (Watch the old Orson Welles flick, The Third
Man). Here the individual is higher than the universal.

Its hard for me to "get."

I cannot accept a philosophy that is based on the existence of God so
easily. (Doesn't matter if I believe in God or not it just seems cheap). So,
the surface reading is about god but I think when explored in depth its
about ethics relating to other people. Not that I'm accepting Kierkegaard.
Writing this made it make a little more sense to me though. Sorry if it
doesn't make sense to you. That is, my writing.

suspension of the ethical, and I'd NEVER.
