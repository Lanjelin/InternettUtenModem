The Subject: Re: living in the universal
At 20:02:44 on 09/09/96, J. Climacus (brettdel@mail.aros.net) wrote:

Well, I'm not really sure whether I'm addressing an individual or
the generic Christian faith; but in any event, a few observations.
First, I'm wondering how we are to take the Abraham/Isaac story
if we believe that god would never command us to break one of his
laws. Did Abraham know that? Was he just pretending to be ready to kill
Isaac, knowing the whole time that, as you say, god would never really
ask him to do something so heinous? And, if so, what kind of a test
is that? We imagine Abraham, tongue firmly in cheek, grinning to
himself and perhaps feeling like he's humoring the big Guy. This is not
exactly a teleological suspension of the ethical. It is really just
play acting, sort of like the bland re-enactments of the transubstantion
playing everywhere at a cathedral near you.
And, besides, if god knows everything, doesn't he know how Abraham
will react? Usually, when a teacher gives a test, it's to find out
what you know.
Criminals, you say? I would doubtless agree, but how do they differ
from Abraham? How does one distinguish the words of the true god from
the posers who are tempting you to do something fiendish? Isn't one
burning bush like any other? Oh, but you say they should know that it
couldn't be god if you're being told to do something so patently wrong.
Well, but what about Abraham? Wasn't that the real god speaking?

As for comparing a forbidden dance (comic relief, I realize) to
murdering Isaac, I think there is at least one important difference.
Doing the lambada or even the twist for that matter involves only oneself; th
the consequences of breaking that law involve only the lawbreaker.
Killing Isaac involves the sacrifice of an innocent. I would feel less
cynical of an order from a god that, however wrong it may be, involved
harm only to myself.

As for hanging out with god, I was making an attempt to be jocular,
which is maybe a little risky when the subject is Kierkegaard (although
I think SK might appreciate it). Be that as it may, I have to ask:
If god is so perfect, wouldn't he have a perfect a sense of humor as
well? ("I could only believe in a god who could dance.") Wouldn't he
be the life of the party, someone we'd WANT to hang out with? And
even if having a divine sense of humor is not part of the godhead,
isn't it true that there are a few prerequisites to worship? If we
are satisfied that we are dealing with the god of our fathers, is that
enough for us to bow down? What if this god also seems to have some
very wrongheaded notions (e.g., like ordering people to kill their
only sons)? My point is that it seems that in order to worship, one
must first respect, love and admire. God must earn our respect. And if
he does, then we might first want to hang with him. And then we can
at least discuss the possibility of worship.
