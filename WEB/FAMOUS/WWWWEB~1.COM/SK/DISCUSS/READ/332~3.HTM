The key issue here is that the "unpleasant alternative" hinted at is
a sinful alternative. The offense SK and the Bible talk about has its
root in the inability of our minds to comprehend the wonderfulness of
what God has done for us. This is "true offense", and the person is
blessed who does not reject God on account of his inability to
comprehend how God could do such a thing. The paradigm case of offense
for SK is the Incarnation, as discussed in the Philosophical Fragments
and the Postscript. God loves us to such a degree that He does the
apparently impossible: The eternal becomes temporal, pure spirit
becomes flesh. Jesus is the "occasion of offense" because he is the
concrete manifestation of God's love, a love that is an offense to
our reason because it transcends it.

Mary is the first individual who is blessed because she does not take
offense. At the angel's announcement of the Virgin Birth (Luke 1:26-38) Mary expresses her puzzlement that she could become pregnant
without intercourse. This is the "occasion" of offense because Mary is
called on to suspend her reason and trust to God that He can do such
a wonderful thing. Mary shows she is blessed because rather than taking offense ("Get out of here angel, with your ridiculous notion!) she responds positively to God: "Let it be done to me as you say." (Luke 1:38).

The notion you are floating would have "offense" based on the fact
that God came into the world through sin - to wit, Mary's engaging in adultery. And it certainly would be offensive if God, in order to
conquer sin in the world, chose sin as the method by which He did it.
This would lead us to an "ends justifies the means" moral philosophy,
which is certainly something SK rejected (see "Purity of Heart.")

Taking offense is not always bad. The true lover believes all good
things about his beloved (see "Works of Love") and takes offense at
the suggestion that his beloved is not pure. This is a healthy offense
and is a sign of genuine love - "love believes all things." So it
is entirely appropriate for us to be offended at the notion that Jesus, our beloved, was the result of sin rather than Mary's loving response to God.

The offense to be avoided is one that is rooted in putting limits on
love and the good. We can't believe that God would be so wonderful as
to take the form of man in Jesus, so we reject it as "irrational". Or
we can't believe that Mary would have such faith that she could say YES to God when the angel proposes the Virgin Birth, so we reject it as "myth". This is an offense that believes too little in love rather than too much.

David Tye
