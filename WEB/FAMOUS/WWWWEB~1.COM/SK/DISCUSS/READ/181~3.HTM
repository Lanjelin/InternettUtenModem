At 10:55:40 on 11/11/96, Christopher Harless (charless@expert.cc.purdue.edu) wrote:

At 14:16:09 PST on 11-08-96, T. Dean wrote:


>>What do you mean in your distinction between the absolute and the universal? (I have not yet read [i]Fear and Trembling[i]). . . .
>>
>I hope I've got this right -- the universal is a realm of reality (my words, not SK's) shared by all, expressed in language, which encompasses a certain amount of truth. Religious doctrine and imperatives (thou shalt not kill) are in the realm of the universal.
>
>The realm of the absolute is different, and unique to each person. Abraham, when he responded to God's command to sacrifice Isaac, was no longer obeying the universal's rules, but he was properly entering the realm of the absolute.
>
>In my own mind, I connect these ideas to Robert Pirsig's description of quality in Zen and the Art of Motorcycle Maintenance (I am addicted to these best-selling American philosophers, I'm afraid). For Pirsig, some aspects of quality (RP's word for the Platonic Idea of the Good) are not communicable to others -- they are things you can only really learn from experience. I think SK's absolute shares something with this category of quality.

Great news! I got my paper done! You can read it at:
http://expert.cc.purdue.edu/~charless/kierkegaard.htm

Unfortunately, though, I did not have time to consult Walker Percy. Basically, I tried to argue that Christian faith, for Kierkegaard, entails belief in the historical event of Christ. I make a distinction between the moment of Christ in the faithful individual, and the moment of Christ as an historical event located in an other individual. The latter is logically prior. Therefore, faith must assume the existence of the one we refer to as Jesus of Nazareth.

I like your idea of linking Kierkegaard's absolute to Pirsig's quality. Certainly they have similarities. Pirsig's book was a life changer for me. I read it on my own while I was taking my first Philosophy class. Suddenly all the language, categories, concepts, etc. took on a new significance as I realized that Pirsig's quest, in a way, is my own, indeed is everyone's (or could be). As a Christian, it is very difficult for me to keep concepts like quality from being directly linked to my idea of God. Of course, theology's task is to consider the connection.
