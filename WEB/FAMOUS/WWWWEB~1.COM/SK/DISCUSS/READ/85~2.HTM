The Subject: Re: living in the universal
At 19:14:04 on 09/07/96, D. B. Hansen (bhansen@foxnet.net) wrote:

At 11:08:41 PST on 08-18-96, J. Climacus wrote:

>I think this may be a correct interpretation of Kierkegaard, and while I'm with
>Kierkegaard on many issues, this is, I think, one where we part ways.
>Although I speak as an outsider on matters of faith, I can't help but
>think that any convictions arrived at via the transcendence of faith
>must still be weighed against the ethical/universal demands of society/
>and/or one's own ethical sensibilities. It has to be so. Our prisons
>are filled examples of people who, in their own estimation, heeded the
>call of god -- people who killed their children because they believes
>it was god's will. We rightly condemn these people. But why? Well, we
>want to say it's because they were wrong -- that they really were not
>acting on god's orders. But how do we know that if the example of Abraham
>is to be taken seriously? It it was a test of faith, did these folks
>pass of flunk? Or is it that they heard god rightly when he gave the
>initial edict to kill, but weren't listening when he later rescinded
>the order?
> I simply could not believe in a god who would order me to kill my son. Even if he didn't mean it, even
>if it was a test, it shows a callousness that strikes me
>as somewhat less than divine. It makes god into a mean-spirited
>practical joker. Which, at least by some Old Testament accounts, may
>be somewhat accurate. (Job, for instance.) Such a god is much more interesting, because more
>human, but not someone I'd want to hang out with.

For the sake of the Judeo-Christian faith, I feel that I should point
out that God has been defined (so to speak) as benevolent, omnipotent,
omniscient, etc., which makes it very easy to dismiss these people to whom
you refer that have filled our prisons by committing crimes by edict of God.
The Christian God would never cause a human being to hurt another human being,
simply because God would be violating his own laws of ethic. These criminals
were, from a Christian standpoint, mistaken. It's as simple as that. You might
say they were coersed by the Devil, who knows.

You see, it is very important to see that Abraham did not kill his son because
God stopped him. These criminals you talk of did kill their children, which is
something God would not allow.

For the sake of argument, it is also important to acknowledge that Abraham placed
the word of God above everything else. It could be said that there would be no
difference between God asking Abraham to offer his son in sacrifice or to dance
the lambada (that's the forbidden dance!), because Abraham would have done
either without question as an act of faith. This is faith that most cannot
concieve of. This is faith that required you to put God first in all things.
This was the faith of Abraham, thus an example to the rest of humankind. This is
the faith of SK's third stage of existence.

As for not hearing the cancelling order, if God were truly talking to you, you would
most certainly hear.

God is allowed to be callous. He supposedly created the world and you.

God is someone you are supposed to worship, not hang out with.
