At 07:52:18 PST on 11-07-96, J. Climacus wrote:

>At 21:47:07 PST on 11-06-96, Responsibility monger wrote:
>
>>A lot of people believe and follow in prolific writings of Soren Kirkegard, but I fail to see through his idiosyncratic logic, after all, how could the fundamental idealism of human action lead to such analogies of bachelorhood and marriage? I myself favor John Sarte's philosophy on people being solely responsible for their actions. I did not fail one of my tests because I did not have time to study, I failed the test because I did not manage my time correctly. This type of reasoning is clear and forthright. What does batchelorhood have to do with my test.
>
>A "true" existentialist? Of course, SK would never have wanted to be confined to any "ism," so in that sense you're right. But Sartre read and admired Kierkegaard and appropriated the basic tenor of his philosophy. The decisive fork in the road is the leap of faith. I think SK would admire Sartre's ability and willingness to flesh out the absurdity of belief and the utter pointlessness of human life. The difference is that SK would insist that the correct response to the absurdity is to have faith; Sartre would simply suggest that we buck-up. As for responsibility, I would have to say that SK expected as much of individuals as Sartre, although certainly in different ways. SK wanted people to think for themselves, to not allow their ideas to be subsumed either by the predominant religion of his day or by the tentacles of Hegel's system. I think SK would say you are responsible for flunking your test (was it philosophy 101, by the way?).

Well said! Kierkegaard would say that Sartre lacked faith because of an offense to the paradox of Christianity. Could it be said that Sartre has a sort of faith, anyway. Faith in human potential?
