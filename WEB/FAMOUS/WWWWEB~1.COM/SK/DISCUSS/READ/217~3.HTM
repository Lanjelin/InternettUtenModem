All right, I've gone back to the text, and I have a couple of quibbles with your take on anxiety. (1) I may, of course, be misunderstanding SK, but he seems to identify, and maybe even equate, anxiety with sinfulness (as opposed to sin itself). So, if sexual desire is bound up with anxiety, it can't be far from sinfulness.

(2) More importantly, I think anxiety as pre-awareness of sensuality is dangerous to the value of The Concept of Anxiety, and threatens the coherence of SK's articulated world view. As discussed in my earlier post, most philosophers from Plato to Nietzsche (and orthodox theolgogy) agree that humanity's consistent tinge of megolomania is its primary failing. The link to (a) despair and (b) faith is clear if anxiety is grounded in the fear that my will to power (to coin a phrase) is going to be wrongly directed, and despair is my utter failure to choose a direction, or even to be conscious that there is a right direction for my will. SK, however, says anxiety is primarily a reaction to a *bodily* inclination. We then have to analogize it to the will (as Mr. Tillett did in his post) to make a coherent link between anxiety and human failing. This bodily basis for anxiety is disconnected from SK's repeated teachings about human failing as despair, faith as virtue, the universal, the aesthetic, etc. Anxiety doesn't seem necessarily relevant to despair.

Am I right to think The Concept of Anxiety ends up just being a detour from SK's central teachings like faith and despair, the ones that cropped up every time he set pen to paper? Doesn't his whole oevre seem to require anxiety to be linked more closely to the will, and specifically the will to power? Or can anxiety as an essentially bodily experience be integrated into the rest of SK?

Any thoughts on how anxiety as sensuality can be integrated into SK's "central" teachings would be greatly appreciated. Alternatively, any discussions of why I'm dead wrong, and anxiety should not be susceptible of integration into SK's line of thought in The Sickness unto Death will also be helpful.
