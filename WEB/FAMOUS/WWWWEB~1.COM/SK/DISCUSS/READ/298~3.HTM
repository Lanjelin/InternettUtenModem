The Subject: Re: Faith and Historical Fact
At 23:48:41 on 01/05/97, C,Cortese (cortese@mail.utexas.edu) wrote:



>But all existence is given, by definition. SK's existence
category is precisely that which is external to us/our subjectivity,
given to us, outside of our control. As such, that things are given
is not a miracle but a fact/presupposition of our peculiar
circumstances. Your "miracle" category, I think, is not
that things are given to us, but that they are given to
us as objectively meaningful experiences. But this is
precisely the discussion we are having: is the meaning of
this objective thing in the object itself, or is it in
my own subjectivity? And if either is the case, what is
the relationship between my subjectivity and objectivity?
"Miracle" describes the problem; I do not think it is a
solution in a philosophic sense (although it could well be
a solution in a personal sense).
>
>As above, I think that if things carry the weight of their
meaning with them, ie your miracle has occurred, then there
is little place left for subjectivity, nor for a sure sense
of what would constitute a concrete individual. Subjectivity
becomes a mere accidental receiving point of an objective
manifestation. So too, inasmuch as the meaning of the
objective is "outside of us," then we are un-free: we can
only accept the inevitable. But note too that we too are
existing. In this sense, we have an aspect of ourselves which
is objective to us. If our own meaning is carried in this
objective part of us, then we are un-free with respect to
ourselves, also.
>
>Now, the SK formulation is: in contemplation mode (which is the
mode we are in in this discussion), any determination of the
meaning of an objective given is a subjective approximation.
In ethical/active mode, any determination of one's own responsibility
to an objective given is a subjective certainty. In the
religious/active mode, any determination of one's own
responsibility to an objective given is a subjective
certainty as a response to a gift from God. In all of these,
the relation of subjectivity to objectivity is given, but
freedom is not lost, or so SK wants to say.
>

>I like your statement that Socrates "was being repelled."
This is a neat way of putting it. SK describes Socrates as
infinite negativity, which carries the connotation of a
totally subjective act on Socrates part. But in truth, a
commitment to ignorance seems to have just as much
acquiescence to being repelled, as it does to an internal
subjective commitment. Whence would come such a negative
commitment without a clear vision as to that which is being
rejected? This clear vision would be very close to "being repelled."

>But again, in what sense is "miracle" a solution?
In what sense, upon the occurrence of the miracle, is
there room for subjectivity? I do not mean to be obtuse
here, so please be patient.
>


I apologise for harping so long on a single word. First it was "lard"
and now I am doing the same with "miracle". Both of these words, have
acted as my archimedean point of departure and have launched me out
into our conversation, which has covered quite a bit of ground, and
been enjoyable -- and I do not consider you obtuse. And since you ask,
I'll try again to explain.


Lard was the problem. Specifically, Mr. Dean wanted to get rid of the
lard, he wanted to stop smearing lard on his goggles so that he could
get a clear view at the objective reality given us. He vehemently
wanted to get rid of subjectivity. And, I think, Mr. Dean, can be said
share the same basic desire as the noble man stuck in Plato's cave -- to
see the light.

So, in comes the difficulty.

OK, well, according to the definiton of subjectivity -- that subjectivity
is the intersection between a consciousness and objective reality
-- I came to the conclusion that if consciousness wants to come into
contact with objective reality then subjectivity cannot be gotten
rid of except by virtue of a miracle.


The difficulty is in holding that two contradictory sentences are both
true -- that is, the difficulty is precisely the paradox (as you
have already pointed out in your last post), its paradoxical that this
can happen -- and to solve the difficulty would be a "miracle".


So, according to our text, we are in agreement -- "the miracle" is
a problematic solution to the problem of lard.

looked passed it -- is there an unproblematic solution to be found?
