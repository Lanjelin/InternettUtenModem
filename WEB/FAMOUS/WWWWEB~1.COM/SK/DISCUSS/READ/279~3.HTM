>>To your question: I do not think SK's leap is the same as the barrier between the subjective and objective in Plato, Kant, et al. To the contrary, I think SK intended something quite different. In Plato, the effort was to disregard the appearances of things and return via a recollection into the formal ideas. In Kant, the effort was to disregard the illusory habits of the mind which push the personality to say something about things in themselves, and to be satisfied with the phenomenal appearances. In neither of these is there a leap to fuse the subjective and objective, but only a program to opt for one side or the other.
>
>But in SK, the leap marks a fundamental change of the personality in its relation to the world, and is a bona fide attempt by SK to formulate, I think, the way in which a fusion of subjectivity and objectivity takes place. It is in the leap that the personality transcends the self-absorption of an ironic esthetic relation to the world, and becomes the self-actualized ethical individual. It is also in the leap that the personality leaves aside the self-actualization of the ethical, and becomes the self-negating religious individual in an absolute relation to the Absolute. In both of these leaps, there is a fusion of the subjective committed personality with the objective: in the case of the ethical individual, the subjective commitment is fused with the objective existing self, and the self is thereby made actual in time (hence SK's famous statement: "truth is subjectivity ... "). In the religious commitment, the subjective commitment of faith to Jesus Christ gives rise to the actuality of the self and the self's relation to the world as a gift from God; aka the "second immediacy" of Fear and Trembling.
>>
>>The leap is also for SK a conceptual presupposition, which he feels is necessary to preserve the freedom of the individual in making the transitions. That one must leap means that the transitions from stage to stage are "beyond reason," in that the individual is not moving through the stages by virtue of a reason, but by virtue of a passionate commitment.
