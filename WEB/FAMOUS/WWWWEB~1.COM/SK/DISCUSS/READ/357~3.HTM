At 14:19:00 on 02/07/97, David Tye (tye@datacube.com) wrote:

At 12:58:58 PST on 02-07-97, Cortese wrote:

>The distinction between the two usages of the word "offence" works through nicely.
>
>Wouldn't it escalate the "offense" (in SK usage) if Jesus was subject to Original Sin? And counterclockwise, doesn't it mitigate the "offence" to exempt him from it, making the entire occurance more palattable(sp) to our understanding?
>

It depends on which understanding you are talking about. To the worldly understanding, all of the major Christian doctrines are unpalattable by their very nature. If you are talking about the divine understanding, we only have access to this via faith so the category of offense doesn't arise since a Christian doesn't "understand" doctrines such as Original Sin, he just believes them.

Perhaps what you are getting at is that Christians would be scandalized by the suggestion that Jesus is subject to Original Sin. This is true, but this scandalized response is, I believe, something very different from Kierkegaard's category of offense.
