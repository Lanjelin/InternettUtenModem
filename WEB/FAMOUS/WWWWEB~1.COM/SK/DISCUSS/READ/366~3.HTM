At 04:13:11 PST on 02-09-97, Frank Lynch wrote:

>You know, I'd spent a lot of time on the meaning of 'offense' while reading PIC. Consulted a number of biblical translations, the OED, etc., ... There are other NT passages which are frequently translated as "a stumbling block" (i.e., rebuke to the understanding) but because this passage was not one of them, I thought it was more in the moral scandal nature.
>
>But be that as it may, I'm not sure that it matters that mcuh to how we read SK?
>
>Frank

Perhaps. What gives me pause is that everything I have read of SK leads me to believe that he was entirely orthodox in his Christian beliefs. I know of no specific references to the Virgin Birth in his writings, but in every other case he defers to the "authorities" with respect to the doctrinal content of Christian faith.

We should remember that SK wrote as one "without authority." His task was not to challenge or redefine orthodox Christian faith, but to wake people up to the subjective significance of that faith. The question he answered was: "Given that you are or wish to be a Christian, how should this fact affect your existence?" For SK, the content of the faith was a given to be defined by the authorities.

My impression is that it would be very unlike Kierkegaard to challenge a traditional doctrine like the Virgin Birth. He would be more likely to ask the question: "Given that the Virgin Birth is true, how does this fact affect your existence?"

Doctrines like the Virgin Birth are not all that subjectively significant, which may be why SK never took the time to address them. His task was to show how Christianity stands as an existential challenge to every individual, so he only addressed those aspects of Christianity that are essential to the existential challenge. The VB just doesn't happen to be one of them.
