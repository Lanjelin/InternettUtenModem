At 05:45:09 on 01/24/97, Tom Dean (tsd@ogk.com) wrote:

At 05:15:08 PST on 01-24-97, Thomas Tillett wrote:

>
>But there most certainly is a consciousness of standing outside of oneself without referent - in what might be called pure negation of the universal, of immanence. "Alienation" might be a term in various contexts which explicates this consciousness; "depression" sometimes maybe also; and of course sin/despair in Sickness Unto Death and faith in Fear and Trembling are specific renditions of this consciousness. . . .

There are many interesting points in your post that I hope to address when the pace of work permits -- I wanted to say preliminarily that I'm afraid we disagree on the above point, and in order to prevent it from becoming a stumbling block to further discussion, I wanted to state my view in a less extreme way.

In the posts on faith and objectivity, I took the view that human subjectivity is always tainted by distortions of perception imposed on us by our objectivity (Mr. Cortese dubbed it the "lard" issue). My argument now that there is no pure human transcendence is another mode of the same view. I don't dispute that at times we stand more outside ourselves than inside, or that a human might cease to perceive external referents, and so experience something _like_ pure transcendence. What troubles me is the lack of incarnation in SK's metaphysics -- Christianity is an incarnational religion and SK is generally seen as a Christian philospher.

Many schools of philosophy have discounted, and even reviled, the human body, but Christianity does not and cannot. (Neither can pure atheism, I think, but this is not my axe to grind). By assuming a chasm between the immanent and the transcendent, however, I believe SK repeats the Cartesian error of not understanding that the ghost _is_ the machine, in a fundamental way, and discounts the importance of incarnation..

My dispute with SK, and it appears with you, is not whether a human can have a transcendental experience, but whether such an experience in a human has meaning without reference to the immanent.
