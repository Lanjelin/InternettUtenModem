>I was speaking of "moods" in the sense of those ramdom emotions arising essentially from our personal biological circumstances. In a rough sense, I am referring to those moods we experience because of our accidental historical, bio-chemical, and genetic make-up. These are moods that we experience as given to us rather than that we create or make. As random, these emotion would be not understandable, by definition.
>
>I think one of the focuses Kierkegaard had was that he tried to precisely distinguish between different emotions. He did this in part based on a view that our life circumstances are essentially random or contingent, and that our accidental circumstances includes a set of given emotional states which are peculiar to each of us personally. The distinction to be made between moods, then, he made based on the type of response we would each make to these random circumstances. And in the ethical and religious, as opposed to the esthetic, the response to changing life circumstances is that the individual will be unchanging; that he will be resolved to hold fast to that which he has committed to.
>
>For example, Judge William was ethically committed to his wife. In this, he recognized completely the contingent nature of his first love for her: perhaps when they met the light was right, his mood happened to coincide with hers, she might of unconsciously reminded him of someone else he loved or respected. But in any case, the original circumstances do not matter to him now, for he has committed to her. And this committment means not just to hold onto a legal relationship, but to hold onto his original mood, his original love of her. Thus, even if she changes, even if she becomes someone different then she was when he married her, as so often happens in a marriage, he will affirmatively hold fast to his original love, now and forever.
>
>In his journal entries, K stated that he might write about what he called "the most beautiful and noble, humanly speaking, forms of despair ... " One of these forms was grief. I puzzled over this for a good while. Why grief? What is it about grief that makes it so much higher than other human emotions? I now think it relates to the point I just made. Suppose, for example, that Judge Williams wife died. What would happen to his commitment to hold onto his original mood, his original love for her? Well, his commitment would still remain, but the content would change to grief. It is precisely the fact that he maintains the commitment to remember his love of her that makes him grieve. Grief is the emotion which remembers a lost loved one, not just as a memory, but as one who was treasured, and still is in the remembrance. And so, Judge William, who committed to love her forever, will now, if he is true and unchangeable in that committment, grieve for her.
>
>I think the poetic moods you are referring to K would categorize as esthetic-ironic in nature. He seemed to have a strong sense of the power of the poet to take his own random, accidental life circumstances and moods and idealize them into a recollection which not only made him able to communicate his personal insights to others, but also made his moods capable of being maintained or repeated at will.

I will respond to your last item first so as to let you know
that I have read your entire letter with great detail and
attention. I don't quite understand your question. If they
are formatted correctly, then what exactly is the problem when
you indicate "...it must be my browser..."? (Feel free to reply
to this agenda via e-mail so as not to abuse the forum; However,
in a short note, I use a curser return deliberately at each sentence (about 64 characters long) so as to control the formatting. What
you see when reading an "already posted post" as opposed to reading
that same post in "response mode" is a matter of how the browser
and/or web site formats full strings of text in "reading mode" and
then again in "response mode." That is why I deliberately use a

Now please take this as a heartfelt reply: You speak of the
emotions, at least in my mind, as if you are speaking of greek
or chinese (neither of which I know) or as if you are speaking
of advanced chemistry or higher mathematics (neither of which
I am minutely versed in either). Your relationship, or should
I say description, is totally foreign to me. But please don't
expound further, for I am NOT contesting your view or your mental
mode of construction here.

Given (moods) verses created or made, which results from
accidental historical, bio-genetic, and genetic make-up
sounds not only strangely artificial, but it also sounds
like a superficiality contrived means to explain what one
being is unable or unwilling to fruitfully live/relive within
him/herself. Without defying or denying a broad-minded
scientifically based credence to the manifestation of myriad
streams of consciousness, of which moods are indeed not a small
part, where is the capacity to fathom, feel, and to be illumined
by the innumerable intricacies of the soul? I don't think you
will find this in the science books, yet there is a plethora of
"information" (poor choice of word) by which these evanescent
subtleties can be more thoroughly and humanly explained (or should
I say lived?).

For one thing, the "spirit of the poet" has more to offer than
a mere codification or the ability to "take his own random,
accidental life circumstances and moods and idealize them
into a recollection..." The poet imbues feeling with the
intimate livingness of being, and not for him/herself, but
for his readers, too, if he is indeed a true poet.

What is "given" is a matter of bio-chemical, physical, and
temporally based ingredients animated by flame or spirit.
Neither the former or latter alone (or even together) can
fully define the ontology of the human species, let alone
being, but the latter is the essence of the field from which
the former has its provenance, hence a more in-depth,
picturesque panorama of the human subject in question.

What we "create and make up" is another visceral realm
from which a material study alone will never suffice,
especially and specifically because it engenders the
arena of mind, will, intellect, and imagination, together
with bio-chemical and physical make-up. With respect to
"moods," no fine line can be drawn here, nor should one be:
Moods are what we feel and hopefully understand by "living
them"; they, the moods, are not digested or fathomed, whether
random or contingent (this is another writing), by the
banal means of this or that of the trite psychologies alone.
And they communicated to others best within the realm of
aesthetics, meaning the poetics of being: The moods are most
definitely understandable whether random or contingent. My
research (literary endeavors) bears witness to this most
definitively.

I will not argue Kierkegaard's views here, but merely attest
to what has been lived through the life of my being. But do I
hear you espouse the direct relationship of random to the
aesthetic and contingent to the ethical or religious
(paragraph 2)?

Put Judge William aside for a moment. Tell me about YOU!
What are your personal experiences concerning YOUR contingent
moods that are unchanging? What have you lived here that I
may learn from?

Kindly site for me K's journal entries concerning ..."forms
of despair ..." (paragraph 4). "What is it about grief that
makes it so much higher than other human emotions?" you ask.
Then you continue, "Suppose, for example, that Judge William's
wife died...." No! I will not suppose. Tell what YOU know
of grief.

Without having truly grieved in terms of the loss of a living,
loved mate, I grieve deeply for loss of full Self, yet this
grieving is not unchangeable -- not because it is a random or
contingent mood, but rather because of the dynamic nature of
being. Am I correct? wrong? Is this a contingent mood or random
one? It is ethical/religious or aesthetic? Does this coincide
with Judge William or must I really lose a true, life-long love
to "learn" or live this as you describe it? I know only what I
have lived and feel what I have lived -- hence share the feeling
of "mood" through the scribe of poetry, not science.

"But I go on too long. I hope some of this makes sense."
No. You have not gone long enough. Yes, it makes sense,
but not reverie.

ALP, 9/22/96 2:2 (Book 37)
