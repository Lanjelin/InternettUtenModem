The Subject: Sin is Defiance
At 09:50:27 on 02/06/97, Patrick Lysons (plysons@ocrs.com) wrote:

Sin is defiance. In the context through which and by which K. asserts the despair of the self in despair is in a state of assertive separation from God. The idea of an immanent, ineffable God Who is eternal being involved in the affairs of a temporal, declensive world, is, as K. pointed out, "a logical absurdity." This means that: A) sin is the result of defiance--a willful declension or decision to be apart from God; B) a willingness to continue in that decision (i.e., to be "in" the world in despair) and C) the despair itself implies the absence of God.
K.'s brilliance, if you will, comes from a thoughtfulness that considers the condition of man as a totality of despair. It is absurd to petition a God you have yourself rejected. This means that the world is not the result of God's creation, but the result of a decision of the mind. "He began with the infinite abstraction of the self,and now he has finally become so concrete that it would be impossible to become eternal in that sense; nevertheless, he wills in despair to be himself. What demonic madness--the thought that most infuriates him is that eternity could get the notion to deprive him of his misery (Sickness Unto Death, p. 72)."
Working through Kierkegaard is working through the original existential deconstructionist. If sin is "real" it must be eternal; thus, it is the role of victim and victimizer that won't be relinquished by the sufferer. Because he is so far from the idea of eternity, it doesn't occur to him that sin is no more than a mistaken formation in the mind ("inwardness" in K.'s terms).
