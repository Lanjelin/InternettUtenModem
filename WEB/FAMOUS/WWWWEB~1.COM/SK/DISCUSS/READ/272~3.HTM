>This conversation seems to involve one of the differences I perceive between SK and the other existentialists. Generally, the existentialist is free to define value (even to create it), and so "freedom" includes deciding what is desirable for oneself. That is, "value" (as Walter Kaufman uses the term in his Nietzsche translations) is self-determined, insofar as existentialism recognizes it.
>
>SK cannot take this view, because his metaphysics includes an Absolute, from which (and perhaps by which) values are determined specifically for the single individual. The individual may make the interior motion of the knight of resignation, or alternatively the leap of faith, but the individual may not decide what it is that will constitute the leap of faith (the motion an individual makes either is or isnt the leap of faith), and one may not make both motions at once. The Absolute functions on the value of the motion as an external determination.
>
>Thus, for SK, value is objective (an external determinative force) and not subjective (an internal subjective motion) -- or so I have argued in my discussion of faith and objectivity. I think that if, as C. Cortese indicates, SK left some window open for free self-defining activity -- for value-determination -- Mr. Cortese's text also proves that the external determination of the absolute may decide to preclude that freedom. Again, SK would NOT say that the individual is free to create value with respect to actions demanded of the individual. So freedom only occurs in the interstices.
>
>For this reason, I do not think the "freedom" of the leap of faith is the same as Heidegger's "freedom." (My recollection of MH's thought is that, except for "thrown-ness," the human individual is entirely self-defining and value-determinative. I confess I have no idea what MH meant if and when he said "freedom is freedom unto death," unless that was during the period he was developing t-shirt slogans for the SS).
>
>All this being said, Mr.Paproski is, I think, right to see the leap of faith as free -- in one sense. The leap is not externally determined, but is a completely interior movement in response to an external determination. On the other hand, the meaning of the leap and the manner in which the leap must be made _is_ externally determined because the value of the leap is created by the absolute, not by the subject.
>
>So, Ms. Roces, I agree that SK does seem precluded from teaching the same "freedom" as a Camus or a Sartre. In fact, doesn't he seem to have inverted the meaning of freedom, by defining a freedom that exists only in the absence of compulsion? Nietzsche's criticism that herd morality inverts "good and bad" thus seems to be borne out. I am more than a little troubled by this reflection.
