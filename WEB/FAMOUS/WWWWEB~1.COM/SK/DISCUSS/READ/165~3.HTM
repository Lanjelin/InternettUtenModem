At 23:09:10 on 11/06/96, Robin Hood (charless@expert.cc.pudue.edu) wrote:

At 21:47:07 PST on 11-06-96, Responsibility monger wrote:

>A lot of people believe and follow in prolific writings of Soren Kirkegard, but I fail to see through his idiosyncratic logic, after all, how could the fundamental idealism of human action lead to such analogies of bachelorhood and marriage? I myself favor John Sarte's philosophy on people being solely responsible for their actions. I did not fail one of my tests because I did not have time to study, I failed the test because I did not manage my time correctly. This type of reasoning is clear and forthright. What does batchelorhood have to do with my test.

You obviously have not read Sartre's Critique of Dialectical Reason. Sartre uses dialectical reason, as opposed to analytical reason, which is anything but straight-forward. Kierkegaard also employs dialectics in his analyses of the self in Sickness Unto Death, for example. Both thinkers realize that to make human life intelligible, rational, and meaningful requires what Kierkegaard calls indirect communication.

Kierkegaard would agree that you are responsible for flunking your test. He would also recommend that it is your responsiblity to spell his name correctly. (Sorry, I couldn't resist.) Finally, batchelorhood has everything to do with your test, if that is what you relate youself to in an absolute way.
