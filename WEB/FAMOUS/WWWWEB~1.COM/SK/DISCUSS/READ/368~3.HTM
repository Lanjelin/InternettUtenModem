The Subject: Re: 'Offense' and the Virgin Birth
At 06:55:16 on 02/11/97, Tom Dean (tsd@ogk.com) wrote:

The "offense" of the Incarnation is central, not only in SK's thought, but to Christian theology. The "scandal of particularity" was the subject of the seventh ecumenical council (I think it was Nicea III, but I'm not sure), in which the propriety of making icons was debated hotly. Interestingly enough, the central question was one of idolatry: whether praying before icons was a violation of the Biblical prohibition against making and worshipping "graven images." The council reached a startling conclusion (offensive to many): Since God had become flesh, and looked a particular way, it was not improper to make pictures of Christ. From this it followed that pictures of the saints could be made, and that one could perform worship or prayer in front of the pictures without sinning.

The distinction between praying before icons and praying to icons is a fine one, and many more fundamentialist Christians have rejected it as meaningless. But one could view icons as the equivalent of a pilgrimage. No one imagines that, on going to Jerusalem, God will hear his prayers more clearly -- but only that, after travelling to Jerusalem and reflecting on wht happened in that very place, one will focus one's prayers more clearly.

There are many reasons that this gives offense, as SK uses the term. Not least is that the Incarnation gives God most clearly to a favored few (1st century Palestinians), and to the rest of us at second hand.
