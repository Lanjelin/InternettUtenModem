I have not read the text in some time and do not have time to read it again at this time, so pardon me if I am rather loose in my references. But, I do not recall that SK said that anxiety was primarily a reaction to a bodily inclination. Anxiety does not arise or react to anything; it has no psychological source in the sense that, eg, anger does. It is a foundational presupposition that explains why Adam (or any member of the human race) would leave a state of innocense and become guilty. For the concept of anxiety to function as such a presupposition, it has to be in some sense general enough to cover all cases. Thus, to relate it, either in sympathy with or in a reaction to, something as specific as bodily inclination would be to render it useless as a general explanation.

The presupposition of anxiety is not sin-in-man per se. It explains the possibility of the choice that might give rise to sin. God commands Adam not to eat a certain fruit. In innocense, Adam has no reason to disobey God, which is also to say that assuming a state of innocense, there would be no adequate explanation as to why Adam would disobey such a clear command. Thus, there must be something else present within Adam as a man per se, which sufficiently explains his subsequent disobediance. SK puts forward the concept of anxiety, a "sympathetic antipathy and an autopathetic sympathy;" ie an emotion which is a union of opposite forces. It is a motive force which is essentially ambiguous about an object: God points at the tree of knowledge and says: "Do not eat the fruit thereof." Anxiety is that factor in Adam which is repelled by the fruit, because of the command, but also attracted thereto, but attracted precisely because Adam was commanded to stay away.

In this way, anxiety produces the situation where there is a choice. To repeat, in innocense, Adam would have no choice once he hears God's command. But because he has anxiety, he becomes suddenly aware of a choice: to obey God or not. The fruit becomes attractive precisely because God has forbidden it; the possibility of choosing against God's command thus becomes a real possibility. But to choose wrongly is to lose one's relationship with God, and thereby to lose the freedom that one had before the choice to have a relationship with God. Thus, the fundamental basis of anxiety, SK says, is not fear of any particular object, but the fear of loss of freedom, which is the fundamental fear in every choice.

But in the story of Adam, the specific fear of anxiety is the fear of the loss of a relationship with God by choosing wrongly. And therein is at least in part the relationship of anxiety to despair. The solution to despair, you remember, is that point wherein the subject relates himself to the "power which posits him" ie, to God. Thus despair is the absence (or rejection) of a relation with God. Anxiety, on the other hand, is that capacity within the individual which presents the possibility of not having a relationship with God; thus without it, there is no despair, no consciousness of the absence of a relation. With anxiety, man gains the possibility of despair, but also that which is of supreme value: the power of the freedom to choose between God or himself. If he chooses himself, he has essentially chosen sensuousness, bodily inclination, and all the other temporal capacities which make up a man, but he also gains despair.

I do not find it easy to proof-read and revise my writing in these entry forms. I hope the above is not too much of a mish-mash.
