It seems to me that Existentialism is precisely the pursuit of answering the question, What does it mean for me to exist as an individual? Kierkegaard and others do make use of abstract, ontological, concepts, but the real concern is the individual in his/her concrete existence. Heidegger, for example, makes the well known distinction between the ontological/existential and the ontic/existentiell. We can talk about human existence in abstract ways so long as we don not forget that it must apply to existing humans. That is precisely what Hegel forgot. He stopped with the abstract human "as such." Kierkegaard noticed that Hegel's logic missed the boat because he thought that he could "mediate" the dichotamies in human existence. Kierkegaard seeks to maintain the dichotamies (eg. possibility/necessity, finitude/infinitude) in dialectical tension.

>This is an idea that would require more time and space to do justice. Sartre denies that there is any sort of ontological substance at the base of human consciousness. There is no "thinking thing", ego, transcendental self, etc. All there is is consciousness. Consciousness by itself is empty, it is always consciousness of something. Rather than being, consciousness is more like a negativity. It is presence-to-self. It is like a fissure in being which allows being to present itself to itself. Having said this, he goes on to talk about structures of human consciousness, which I would argue are nevertheless ontological, though perhaps not metaphysical in the classical sense. For more on this see, Being and Nothingness.

Have you just coined a phrase -- "The thing forgotten"? And yes, I would say that the thing forgotten does stand by itself. It stands by itself for the subjective individual (and it is there for the objective individual even though he cannot see it; let the comedy begin). The dificulty begins when the subjective individual tries to communicate this thing forgotten, when he tries to remind people. Precisely by trying to remind others he exposes himself to the dangers of becoming objective himself and forgetting, i.e. becoming positive. Because, you see, in Kierkegaardean terms, I suppose this thing forgotten would be negetive and inward, in contrast to outward/positive religiosity. And the difficulty is the communication of this negetive, which the subjective soul desires to do, but not at the cost of making the negetive into the positive, which often happens right under our noses. The entire postscript is SK shifting positive to negetive, negetive to positive; going through fits of rapture, technical detail, blabbering gibberish, and then back to rapture. Do you know the sentence about the "...good advice, and also quite cheap"? Its in postscript, its an amazing run on...

So, in summary, the thing forgotten stands by itself, but there are those who cannot see it (although they areconstantly looking right at it -- let the comedy begin again), and then there is those who can see it and want to communicate it, helook what i got (and when they say thatits gone)-- and then there are those who learn to live with it, quietly.
