"The unknowable is intelligible by uncanny means."? As usual Tony, you send me scurrying for the dictionary; not that Webster is an authority on the arcane vocabularies of us savants, but it is perhaps a good place to start.

Unknowable: Not knowable; esp : lying beyond the limits of human experience or understanding.
Unknown: Something that requires discovery, identification or clarification
Intelligible: Capable of being understood or comprehended.
Uncanny: Being beyond what is normal or expected.
(Advenient, I must report, does not appear to be a recognized term.)

Once again, I have to say that what is unknowable is just plain unknowable. I think the word you're groping for is "unknown," since this at least allows us to talk about something OUT THERE that is currently under-wraps, but may, if we reach a certain state of enlightenment, be discovered, identified or clarified. And, certainly, many sojourns into the unknown have come through "uncanny means," although I think it is open to question whether the means itself is not what shapes our sense that we've "discovered" something. (I'm thinking paradigm shift, or perhaps even the phenomenon McLuhan (in perhaps his only memorable sound bite) described in the oft-quoted phrase: "The medium is the message.").

I suppose what you're talking about is mystical states, and, once again, I'll buy the notion that there are insights available that are incommensurate with language. But I do not think those insights can rightly be said to be "unknowable" although perhaps unknown or even known only to a select few.

I agree that it is important to state, as clearly as possible, what can be known. I think that in that way we get clearer on that which is not known, maybe even that which is unknowable. But can we really believe that in so doing we have given any "attributes" to this unknowable something-or-other? Even to call it an "unknowable something or other" seems to go beyond what can realistically be said. Perhaps that's why you stick with "the unknowable," brevity giving it at least the appearance of modesty. But how can we even know it's unknowable? How do we know anything is unknowable?

On a final note, it seems quite unlikely to me that it is possible to experience anything outside language and thought (although it certainly seems possible that there are realms outside beyond or at least in addition to "rational" thought). I have a vague recollection of Kierkegaard heaping scorn and ridicule on Hegel for the notion of "absolute thought," by which SK took him to mean that he had somehow forgotten he was a person existing at a certain period of time with pains, appetitites and perhaps even flatulence. Even Hillary Clinton would find it challenging to reach that exalted state of forgetfulness.
