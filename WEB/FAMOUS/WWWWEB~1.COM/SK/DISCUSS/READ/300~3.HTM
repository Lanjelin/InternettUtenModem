I am uneasy intruding into this previously private conversation merely on grounds that my name was called (a prerogative that usually belongs to the devil in melodrama -- not a particularly encouraging precedent). But here goes:

I think Mr. Cortese's "miracle" is exactly that, absent faith in God. The "fusing" or "reconciliation" of subject and object in SK's thought is the natural product of faith. Faith is the context in which SK writes -- his philosophy assumes (or accepts) the existence of the divine, and interaction between the human and the divine. In the light of SK's faith, there is a completely objective objectivity -- reality as God sees it. Absent God, I agree with Mr. Cortese, the synthesis of subject and object which Mr. Tillett has so clearly described (or, described in a manner so faihful to SK and the Christian worldview) is a miracle, or an impossibility.

But, positing God, this fusion is a necessary precondition of metaphysics and ethics -- the human subject (determinor) _is_ an object (determinee, for want of better terms), for the divine -- i.e., God acts as an external determination on every subject, and the freedom of the subject to self-determine is thereby limited. Therefore, subjectivity and objectivity are merged/synthesized/fused/reconciled, in a way which is not possible in an atheistic existential philosophy such as Sartre's. God (and SK) cures the alienation between internal (subjective/determinor) reality and external (objective/determinee) reality. This is part and parcel of SK's underlying assumptions about reality, putting a Creator-God into his a priori assumptions -- his axioms -- about reality.

In both SK's view and the atheistic existentialist views, subjectivity is an inseparable part of the subject's experience of the objective (actuality, as I understand Mr. Tillett to be using the term). In other words, under either metaphysics, "actuality" inevitably carries with it some distortion of the external (lard on our lenses, Mr. Cortese). But in the first, atheistic view, there is no definer/determinor to tell us what is distortion, what is reality, because the only available determinor is humanity. All of humanity's individual members may well share the same distortions of perception, and among them there is no standard of agreement for filtering distortions. Such reconciliation would indeed be a miracle -- a miracle not accepted by any atheistic existentialist -- and atheistic existentialism is therefore generally burdened with an alienation (because of the inevitability of distortion/lard) between internal and external reality.

The Kierkegaardian view provides a subject (determinor) who is not an object also (determinee), in the person of a God whose existence is presumed a priori. God's perceptions suffer from no distortion/lard and a human person who lives in harmony with God's view of things is thus in touch with a truly objective universe -- or a universe where alienation can be eliminated, by proper relationship to God (aka the Absolute).

Am I right, Mr. Cortese, in hearing an echo in your chosen word, "miracle," of the phrase often used in empirical science to ridicule an incomplete hypothesis: "And then a miracle occurs"? But in SK, the miracle is placed, like an axiom in geometry, before the elements of the proof of the hypothesis. SK offers the hope that a miracle _has_ occured, and then reconciles the irreconcilable. May I proffer the word "magic"?
