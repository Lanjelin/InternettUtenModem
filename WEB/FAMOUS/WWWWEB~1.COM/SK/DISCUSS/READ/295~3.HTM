The Subject: Re: Faith and Historical Fact
At 17:49:18 on 01/03/97, Thomas Tillett (tillett@usa.net) wrote:

>
>To use mathematical terms, is actuality the intersection or the union of existential/objetive and the subjective/conceptual? Does actuality contain the dichotomy in its entirety, (which I do not believe is what what you are saying because you said that actuality is smaller than existence by itself, which definately makes it smaller then the combination of existence and the conceptual realm -- unless the conceptual realm is a negetive value), or is actuality just a small portion where the dictotomous terms overlap?
>>
>
>This pulls into the original confusion specifically address in my other responce.
>There is Jesus, who is the truth, the objective truth -- he is there for all of us as the truth.
>There is a subject to believe or take offence at this truth-- this is a subjective interaction and is in turn entirely understandable.
>But to accept him as a given, objectively, this is the miracle.

1. It contains the dichotomous terms in their entirety - as a union. Actuality for SK is not without a single person that positively forges it. Thus, there is no sense to a larger actuality within which a smaller more personal actuality might make itself known.

2. Instead of "miracle," why not use SK's term of "paradox?" Faith is the acceptance of the unacceptable. Thus, faith is defined as the subjective response to such a given as Jesus Christ as God.

You are on to an important point, I think, when you constantly recur to wanting to say, in the "miracle," that the objective given has its truth within it. SK's important thesis about Jesus Christ was that His person, as it was given, was the sign of offense. His givenness then demanded a decision: to be offended or to believe. It is in the latter that a person takes up faith as a task. But this would also be coincident with living, or recognizing in the moment, the miracle: the given person Jesus presented in a moment of time as God. 

Thomas M. Tillett
