The Subject: Re: FREEDOM and KIERKEGAARD
At 06:40:28 on 12/21/96, Bruce Paproski (bpaprosk@netcom.ca) wrote:

At 16:24:36 PST on 12-20-96, C.Cortese wrote:

>"It seems fair that in things permissable everyone should be
>to do what he pleases. An intervention is only accomplished
>only when what one person does will place another under
>obligation to do something. Any expression of displeasure is
>permissable, because it does not intervene with an obligation in
>another person's life. If the crowd thus presents a man with a pereat
>[let him die], There is no intervention whatever in his freedom.
>He is not summoned to do something; nothing is demanded of him.
>He can lounge undisturbed in his living room, smoke his cigar, busy
>himself with his thoughts, jest with his beloved, make himself
>comfortable in his robe, sleep soundly -- indeed, he may even absent
>himself, because his personnal presence is not in the least required.
>But a torchlight procession is a different matter; if the celebrity is
>absent he must come home at once; if he has just lit up a fragrant cigar
>he must put it down at once; if hehas gone to bed he must get up at
>once, scarcely has time to put on his trousers, and must dash bareheaded
>under the open sky to give a speech. What applies to prominent
>individualities in reguard to such manifestations of the populace en masse
>also applies in the same way to us humbler folk on a smaller scale.
>A literary attack, for example, is not an intervention in an author's
>personnal freedom; why should not everyone be able to express his opinion,
>and of course the one attacked can calmly attend to his work, stuff his
>pipe, leave the attack unread, etc. Approval however is more dubious."
>
>(page 7 in the preface of Postscript)
