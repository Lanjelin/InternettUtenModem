>How was he existential? When I think of existentialism I think of Camus or Sartre, two atheists. Existentialism seems here and now, rather Zen, not Christian.

>Please reply


SK's existentialism is a different flavor from Sartre's, but I think he has a recognizably similar view of metaphysics to JPS. SK thinks we come in contact with the divine as an unmediated experience (the single individual's absolute relation with the absolute) -- that is, that experience is the truest connection to the ground of being. SK distinguishes this highest form of faith from dogmatic, mediated faith (the plane or realm of the "universal"). The reality the individual experiences in relation to the absolute is untransmittable, and the relation itself is determined by the sum of the experience. Hence, SK's thought is generally classed as existentialism.

SPS, as I read him, thinks the individual determines the ground of being, or at least he thinks the individual has a lot more to do with determining it than SK does. Both, however, think that the ground of being resides in or is intimately connected with an untransmittable experience, and to that extent I agree that they can be grouped together as existentialists.
