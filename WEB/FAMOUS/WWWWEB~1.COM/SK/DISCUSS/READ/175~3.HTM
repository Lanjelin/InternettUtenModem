I really have never understood how Sartre and Kierkegaard come to be categorized together -- but then, Nietzsche's thought seems as different as can be from both SK's and JPS's, and he is often listed as an existentialist. Can anyone identify the unifying thread, or is "existential" a sort of catch-all title for odd-lot philosophers who have a non-Greek metaphysical outlook?
>
Maybe the absolute for Sartre is nothingness? Ok, this is not well founded and does violence to the distinction between the philosophers. But he does have a concept of the ideal man, and a socio-political ideal, both of which we project through our choices.

How is this?: What characterizes a thinker as existential is the attempt to understand what it means to exist as a human being. You might add as a concrete individual. Sartre would say as a singular universal. Another feature of existentialism is the use of Reason to understand and communicate the paradoxes, absurdities of human existence. This is a special sort of reason, namely dialectical. There are also themes that link existentialists, such as despair, anxiety, human finitude, estrangement and alienation.

The label "existentialist", as with any label is difficult to pin down. One can see existential themes and insights in very unsuspecting locations, therefore as a category of thinkers, or as an independent philosophical tradition, it may have a limited future as the insights proliferate into other disciplines. Someone may disagree. I think what determines whether a thinker gets a label is whether the thinker isolated specific problems for intense investigation. As long as there are people who focus on the above themes, there will be existentialists. But like "humanism" it is likely to undergo so many turns that the term will become meaningless. My personal opinion is that this has not happened yet. The force of existentialism, despite the '70s has yet to be felt, especially in America.
