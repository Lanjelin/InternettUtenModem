The Subject: Re: Faith and Historical Fact
At 13:14:37 on 12/09/96, Tom Dean (tsd@ogk.com) wrote:

At 12:24:02 PST on 12-09-96, Cary Cortese wrote:

>Mr. Dean,
>
>You confused me with this sentence about the realm of the subjective: "the realm of the subjective begins at the intersection of objective facts with a human consciousness, and so creates some confusion".
>
>If you have time, could you would explain this further for me?
>
>Cary Cortese
>
I'm afraid I didn't mean anything terribly deep. Objective facts, as I define them, are events that unfold into a human consciousness, but which exist outside the consciousness. Of course, about 1/30 of a second after we preceive objective facts, they begin to get colored by our very act of perceiving them. We subjects immediately lard subjectivity over the objective. "Snow" on the hillside only remains snow long enough for my consciousness to start making idiosyncratic connections that have very little to do with snow. For example, a Floridian may connect snow (as a rare event) with some portent. E.g., it snowed the day my mother died (not really), and so snow is tied forever to loss and sorrow in my mind. As I use the term "objective," snow has no objective connection to loss and sorrow, although the Floridian subject makes a real connection between several objective events (snow, mother's death, another snowfall), to have a subjective experience (or to create a subjective fact, if you like). The snow's existence, meanwhile, is objective (not dependent on the observer).

But I might think snow is cold in itself -- objectively -- while in fact to a person from a colder planet, snow might burn. Then the subjectivity of "coldness" is revealed. I only missed it because I failed to recognize the border at which I the subject began to interact with, and not simply experience snow. Hence the confusion.
