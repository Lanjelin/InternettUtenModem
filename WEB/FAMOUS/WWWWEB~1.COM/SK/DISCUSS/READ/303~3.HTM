The Subject: Re: Sin and the Function of God
At 13:39:42 on 01/07/97, Tom Dean (tsd@ogk.com) wrote:

At 04:44:22 PST on 01-05-97, Tom Dean wrote:

I think sin is the natural next step from the thread on faith and objectivity (which hasmoved through several stages). Also, I spent too long composing this post to let it get archived immediately. . . Any takers?

>SK says at the beginning of Part Two of _The Sickness unto Death_:
>"Sin is: *before God, or with the conception of God, in despair not to will to be
> oneself, or in despair to will to be oneself.* Thus sin is intensified weakness or
>intensified defiance: Sin is the intensification of despair. The emphasis is on
>*before God*, or with a conception of God; it is the conception of God
>that makes sin dialectically, ethically, and religiously what lawyers call
>'aggravated despair.'"
>
>This definition has always struck me as somewhat circular. It also seems to
>put the job of defining reality and self entirely into "God's" part of the
>equation. This is consistent Christian with orthodoxy -- see, for example,
>Augustine's discussion of sin in his Confessions, where he defines sin
>as simply the failure to say yes to God. For Augustine and SK both, sin is
>the only thing (it seems to me) a person can do, since God is the source of
>all virtue or right action, and a single human individual is faced with a
>choice between rubber stamping the ukase of the Divine (it shall be thus),
>or violating the order of the universe. Yet neither SK nor Augustine
>provide a mechanism for determining what the Divine has decided in individual
>instances. That is, when the good decision is a difficult balancing between
>conflicting duties or values, SK's and Augustine's God seems to me to bow out
>of the picture. The God described in the quote is only good -- it seems to
>me -- for John Wayne type conflicts, when it's obvious what's right, and when
>the challenge of right activity is only to determine one's proper place.
>
>SK's view seems to me altogether too safe and Victorian -- a tea party morality.
>For example, despair as failure to be oneself as one is called to be by God
>seems meaningless in trench warfare, when one has to balance the
>(patriotic) evil of deserting your comrades against the (personal moral) evil of killing
>others, and against the (anti-survival) evil of rotting in a trench. In complicated
>situations, the Absolute demand to be a single individual in exactly one particuar way grows so hard to distinguish that
>I have trouble attaching a meaning to SK's definition of sin. Again, it seems both
>unduly simple, and it seems circular because the failure to find out God's
>will is sin, and yet it is the necessary condition of human existence.
>
>This dovetails into the discussion in the "Faith and Objectivity" discussion
>about subjectivity and objectivity being wed in SK -- It seems to me SK marries
> subject and object at the expense of permitting the subject to define the object,
>at some points. While I remain a rabid SK fan, I really have trouble accepting
>his views of sin. I have railed against his definition of original sin in this forum
>before, and now I am questioning his view of individual despair/sin.
>Any takers?
>
