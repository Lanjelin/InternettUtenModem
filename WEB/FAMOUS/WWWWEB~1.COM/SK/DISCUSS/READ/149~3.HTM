The Subject: Re: Sin
At 11:30:47 on 10/31/96, Tillett (tillett@usa.net) wrote:

At 09:54:14 PST on 10-16-96, J. Climacus wrote:

> Let's talk about sin. According to SK, the ethical implies the possibility of sin, but is powerless to deal with it. In other words, repentence is beyond the scope of the ethical. Does this mean that as long as you stay on the straight and narrow, you have no need to go beyond the ethical? But once you screw up, you need religion and God in order to repent by establishing that one-to-one relationship with God? I'm wondering if you can't expiate sin merely by expanding the universal. For example: It is presumptively wrong to kill, but under these particular circumstances I can (a la Kant) will that the maxim of my action (killing in self defense) become a universal law. And can't you repent by establishing a one-to-one relationship with the one you have wronged without bring God into the conversation.

Its important to note that K plays with two senses of the word "ethical." One is in the sense of the ethical individual, aka the one who is trying to live the ethical life. The other is in the sense of the ethical per se, which is the ideal requirement or standard that the individual is trying to conform himself to. Thus, sin is implied by the ethical standard because the state of sin is in relevant part a failure to adhere, or failure of ability to adhere, to all standards. But the ethical is "powerless to deal with" sin, because the ethical standard by its nature cannot permit any notion to enter in of failure. It stands as an unmitigated or unmitigateable standard of behavior that the ethical individual MUST conform to.

Repentence is thus not a part of the ethical as the ideal requirement, but it is very much a part of the individual who is trying to live ethically. Upon failure, the unyielding demand of the ethical standard is to repent, ie cure the damage done, and return to the task.

I believe that, according to K, Sin is essentially guilt before God, and therefore you couldn't expiate sin through expansion of the universal, or a one-on-one relationship with a person. Its a completely different mode or manner for the individual to live, and thus not reconcileable with strategies appropriate to the ethical (which is not to say that expanding the universal is an appropriate strategy in the ethical. It sounds like a pragmatic mitigation of what is supposed to be an absolute requirement).

Thomas Tillett
