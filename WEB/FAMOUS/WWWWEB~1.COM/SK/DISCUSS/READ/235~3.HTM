Mr. Dean,

Let me show you where I am in my attempt to understand:

"The realm of the subjective begins at the intersection of objective facts with a human consciousness, and so creates some confusion".

So there are:

1. objective facts
which exist independantly of human consciousness
2. a human consciousness
which exists independantly of objective facts

3. an INTERSECTION
these objective facts and a human consciousness

4. confusion
pops up in a human consciousness
created by the intersection with objective facts
due to some intrensic fault located in the human cosciousness

You then you brought in a new term... --PERCEPTION, of objective facts by a "human consciousness"; PERCEPTION being a certain kind of "INTERACTION"...

"Objective facts, as I define them, are events that unfold into a human consciousness, but which exist outside the consciousness. Of course, about 1/30 of a second after we preceive objective facts, they begin to get colored by our very act of perceiving them. We subjects immediately lard subjectivity over the objective."

So, I add #5.

5. There is a time before this INTERSECTION, a magical 1/30th of a second, where the "human consciousness" does not "interact" with the "objective facts" (larding them over with subjectivity), but instead simply "experiences" them (without subjective lard)(and without confusion)(and the intrensic fault with human consciousness disappears).

(I like your use of the term "lard" -- its vivid)

And finally,
The Lard Question:
Am I "experiencing" your posting, or merely "interacting" with it?

>>
>I'm afraid I didn't mean anything terribly deep. Objective facts, as I define them, are events that unfold into a human consciousness, but which exist outside the consciousness. Of course, about 1/30 of a second after we preceive objective facts, they begin to get colored by our very act of perceiving them. We subjects immediately lard subjectivity over the objective. "Snow" on the hillside only remains snow long enough for my consciousness to start making idiosyncratic connections that have very little to do with snow. For example, a Floridian may connect snow (as a rare event) with some portent. E.g., it snowed the day my mother died (not really), and so snow is tied forever to loss and sorrow in my mind. As I use the term "objective," snow has no objective connection to loss and sorrow, although the Floridian subject makes a real connection between several objective events (snow, mother's death, another snowfall), to have a subjective experience (or to create a subjective fact, if you like). The snow's existence, meanwhile, is objective (not dependent on the observer).
>
>But I might think snow is cold in itself -- objectively -- while in fact to a person from a colder planet, snow might burn. Then the subjectivity of "coldness" is revealed. I only missed it because I failed to recognize the border at which I the subject began to interact with, and not simply experience snow. Hence the confusion.
>
