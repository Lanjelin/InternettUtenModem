The Subject: Re: Faith and Historical Fact
At 11:37:45 on 12/05/96, Thomas Tillett (tillett@usa.net) wrote:

At 06:55:37 PST on 12-04-96, Tom Dean wrote:

>A number of posts here in the last few weeks have discussed the link between historical fact and faith. The question, in general terms, has been whether we have to believe that certain ....

In SK, faith is not a communication or communicable at all, but a positive activity of the person. As such, it would fit more nearly into your definition of subjective facts as the uncommunicable.

Faith is an inward commitment rendered unto Jesus the man, as God Himself. Jesus as a man is/was certainly an objective fact, and as a man is as factual as any other person is. But Jesus as God is in no sense objectively determinable or factual. That determination is and must be done faithfully, not factually. Again, this would coincide more nearly with your definition of subjective facts as it relates to the one rendering faith. Jesus is certainly unique, and would thereby constitute a particular fact within your definition, but His uniqueness in this respect derives from His claim to be God. Thus, the essential thing in this "particular fact" is the decision to believe or disbelieve Him. The decision to believe Him is precisely the commitment of faith, and we are back to a subjective activity rather than an objective fact as the ground of the situation.

SK says somewhere that faith, rendered correctly, yields its own object. I think in this statement he is implicitly contrasting faith to an ethical commitment which yields the actuality of the person who is acting ethically, but not the actuality of the object that is his telos. If so, then faith would seem to be more correctly termed personal and subjective than objective or objectively-derived.

These are a few arguements that come to mind. I would be interested in any response or further thoughts you may have on this issue. 

Thomas M. Tillett
