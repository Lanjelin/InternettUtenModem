The Subject: Re: Abraham
At 14:56:28 on 08/23/96, Steve (SteveFisher111@msn.com) wrote:

At 00:12:03 PST on 08-13-96, Kenneth Friedman wrote:

>I believe the Abraham/Isaac story is one that might only be viewed as being the ultimate example of ethical behavior and the relativity of ethics. The term ³ethical behavior² in my mind is a misnomer, and should rather be termed ³cultural behavior,² as the mindset of the peer is the mindset of oneself.
>Abraham was and for all time will be an example of God¹s influence on the thoughts of man, and the willingness of man to choose his ethical philosophy based upon not his own thoughts, but upon others¹, in this case - God¹s.
>

I must say that I very strongly agree with this observation. After I read "Fear & Trembling," I got the impression that SK was trying to attack Hegel's philosophy of the ethical on Hegel's own terms. I really got the impression that, had I sat down with SK and asked him, he would certainly have defined "ethical" as "obeying God." But since Hegel had defined ethics only in the sense of doing what was "right," and had seemingly left out the reference to obeying God, I got the impression that SK was arguing with Hegel using Hegel's own definitions.

As if SK was saying: "Ok, if 'ethics' are to be defined as what is generally accepted as right... well, if that is the case, then we can't say that Abraham, the Father of our faith, was ethical. He was the father of our faith because he chose to obey God rather than be 'ethical.' "

Generally, of course, what is generally/culturally accepted as ethical often does coincide with God's demands for individuals. But in Abraham's case, it did not. Here, SK showed strongly how Hegel's definition of being religious, ie, being ethical, really broke down. In order to be "truly religious", one has to obey God, [ie, do what is TRULY ethical] even if it means becoming "unethical" in the eyes of the world.
