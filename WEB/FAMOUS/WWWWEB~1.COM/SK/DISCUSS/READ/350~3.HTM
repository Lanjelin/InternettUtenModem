>SK means by "offense" something different from our common understanding of the term. We usually mean by "offense" something associated with moral scandal. What SK means by "offense" is "rebuke to the understanding." The occasion for offense occurs when our natural understanding collides with the divine. Since the divine transcends our intellects, it appears to us as incomprehensible. At such moments we are faced as individuals with a decisive question: Will we reject the divine as the absurdity it appears to be (this is taking offense) or humble our intellects in faith (this is responding with faith rather than offense).
>
>With respect to the Incarnation, the offense is based on the fact that one Jesus of Nazareth, the humble carpenter and son of Joseph, claimed to be God Himself. And not just any God, but Yahweh, the God of Abraham, Isaac and Jacob, Lord of the Universe and Creator of all that Is. This claim is the height of absurdity (offense) to the natural understanding. How can a particular man, born in a particular time and place, also be the Creator of time and of place? How is it that the Eternal God could possibly take on a temporal existence?
>
>The basic theme of all of SK's writing was that 1800 years of Christendom had dulled our understanding of the deeply radical nature of the Incarnation. Over the years the radical break that Christianity posited with respect to natural ways of thinking had been forgotten. People came to take the idea that Jesus Christ is God as just one idea among many ideas. SK wished to wake people up to the truly startling character of Christian claims, and to the fact that the Incarnation is not just another idea among others, but one that can only be approached in fear and trembling. The Incarnation is not just a crazy idea, but an idea that is so crazy that it could never occur to the mind of man. And because it seems so crazy, the possibility arises that we will be "offended" when we realize just what the Incarnation entails. Basically offense is resting in the position that the Incarnation is just too ridiculous to believe.
>
