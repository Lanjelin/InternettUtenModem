OK, This is what I mean by "concrete". If you mean to imply that we can not understand human existence at all, much less in terms of subjectivity, I have no answer. It seems to me that Existentialism is precisely the pursuit of answering the question, What does it mean for me to exist as an individual? Kierkegaard and others do make use of abstract, ontological, concepts, but the real concern is the individual in his/her concrete existence. Heidegger, for example, makes the well known distinction between the ontological/existential and the ontic/existentiell. We can talk about human existence in abstract ways so long as we don not forget that it must apply to existing humans. That is precisely what Hegel forgot. He stopped with the abstract human "as such." Kierkegaard noticed that Hegel's logic missed the boat because he thought that he could "mediate" the dichotamies in human existence. Kierkegaard seeks to maintain the dichotamies (eg. possibility/necessity, finitude/infinitude) in dialectical tension.

This is an idea that would require more time and space to do justice. Sartre denies that there is any sort of ontological substance at the base of human consciousness. There is no "thinking thing", ego, transcendental self, etc. All there is is consciousness. Consciousness by itself is empty, it is always consciousness of something. Rather than being, consciousness is more like a negativity. It is presence-to-self. It is like a fissure in being which allows being to present itself to itself. Having said this, he goes on to talk about structures of human consciousness, which I would argue are nevertheless ontological, though perhaps not metaphysical in the classical sense. For more on this see, Being and Nothingness.
