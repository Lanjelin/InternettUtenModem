The Subject: Re: Faith and Historical Fact
At 12:47:22 on 12/26/96, Tom Dean (tsd@ogk.com) wrote:

At 08:34:32 PST on 12-13-96, Cary Cortese wrote:


>. . . I fear that your response would be something akin to the PURE EXPERIENCE that occurs in that magical 1/30th of a second, an idea that Mr. Dean brought forth and later reniged on.
>
I reneged on the magical 1/30th second because it reveals my limitations too baldly. But your idea that we continuously live in the magical moment is too brilliant and paradoxical for me to ignore.

What is memory, and where is the magic moment in a reverie? Memory isn't the magical 1/30th second, because the storage is entirely subjective. But it is the magic moment because its storage is of the objective past (engulfed in subjectivity -- all right, Mr. Cortese, LARDED over with subjectivity), and because the re-presentation is indistinguishable metaphysically from the presentation that causes it. Is memory then an illusion of the objective? A new objective? Subjective? Dove sia? Dove sia? Nessuno sa.

Hume struggles with this idea, Sartre struggles with it, and so does Cassidy (of the Merry Pranksters). Hume cobbles up a distinction without a difference to explain that the objective/subjective divide (he uses other words which I forget) depends on whether the external thing is really happening. But he forgets to consider for whom a thing happens and what "really" means. Sartre uses a mirror analogy -- place an object between two mirrors so that they create an infinite series of images, and look at the reflections. Where is "reality"? It's both obvious and impossible to say. (The mirror is interesting, because the only way to see the infinite reflection is to enter the picture oneself -- something JPS apparently ignores). Cassidy decides that he is always popping along just behind _NOW_ and tries all sorts of experiments to bring himself closer to the absoute instant of experience.

There doesn't seem to be a now, or a distinction between subject and object that we can confortably point to. And yet we know these things when we see them. None succeed in slaying this giant. Shall we embrace nihilism and decide that All is One, that the Atman is the Brahma? Or do we become playful like Nietzsche and hint at what we cannot express? Or accept Wittgenstein's suggestion that whereof one cannot speak, thereof we must be silent?

There is one escape it seems to me -- find a Definer. Find a fixed point, or a primum mobile, an Absolute, from which the meaning of terms can be determined. The difficulty here is that the exercise is meaningless, (oh, I think you guessed it) UNLESS THE FIXED POINT "really" EXISTS. Can we never get ourselves loose from these shackles, Mr. Cortese?
