This is, at the least, an obtrusive subject, offensive to most
scholars.

We must here deal together with notions of relative knowledge and
absolute knowingness much like relative and absolute truth, while,
at the same time, staying with in the domain of human knowledge,
which seriously and demonstratively posits knowledge other than
human.

What is beyond creation in unknowable. By this we signify and give
credence to "something" or "some other" beyond creation or
excluding creation (let's stay within the realm of "usable
language" for a moment). That what is beyond creation is
unknowable can be modified and clarified by saying, "what is beyond
creation is unknowable to the human; hence indicating the potential
of knowingness to/by other than human comprehension. Such a
figment (at the boundary of usable language) is within the domain
of what is humanly knowable...this "some other," or for the
faithful, a God of some sort (we stay away from this for the
moment).

So I arrogantly rephrase the question: How is the human to know of
the possibility of an unknowable that is unknowable to the human,
while retaining human comprehension; and if such comprehension is
possible, is it then truly and rightly unknowable. The answer,
which will appall most scholars, is yes and no!

Our existence conforms to the laws that are confined to its domain,
but is it then infinite or finite? It is infinite possibility
within a finite realm conforming to a domain, and for us, it is
existence. That we posit that which is exterior to existence,
simply as a notion, is an unknowable that is presently unknown, but
which has a presence in human comprehension as a potentiality to be
know, but perhaps not by human means. That it "the other' is
unknowable does not deny its existence or that "otherness" beyond
creation: we simply don't know of "that something" beyond the
boundary which IS by some other means.

So now we have digressed to give attribute to an unknowable entity,
which has caused it to be known is some feeble sense--yet we have
not left the realm of human knowing, and here the venture
continues, or shall I say begins.

I have discussed much in my writing of the mutation of the species,
of binary stars systems, and, poetically, of My Third Birth (in the
sense of humanity, collectively, not individually.) Here I have
embarked upon a path wherein I posit the advenient possibility --
or potentiality -- of knowing, which is not of the human means as
it is presently understood. Surely, however, anything occurring
within or from the human means (the human vehicle) is of human
source, for how can it be otherwise? But there is more to be said
as to what is human as we know it, and what is human as a domain
that is presently unavailable to the babe as human seed: brute.

Now, you will find flaw in this argument as we begin to leave the
realm of language...but at the same time, never digressing from the
field of knowing, or potential knowing. Then, treading clumsily
further, we enter the realm of pure experience, which is human by
virtue of the apprehending (or fathoming) human being, but is not
of human form in light of certain nebulous subtleties related to
human mutation and the potential of some other cognition which is
not strictly human in relativity.

For this we dispense with the tools of constructed language and
enter a realm of unknowable notions not spoken or thought of,
but experienced, hence leading to its knowingness (again from an
apprehending human vehicle).

Yes, sorta knowable; and yes, a claim to map out the boundaries of
coherent discourse by stepping beyond as Wittgenstein says, but
there is no lapse of [internal] discourse or loss of meaning.
Quite the other way around! (I maintain that the unknowable is
intelligible by uncanny means.)

I can not and will not argue your world-view. Exploration of
the unknowable is indeed that: an exploration. When we find some
previously hidden artifacts, what do we do with them? how do we
interpret them in terms of "human" knowledge. I leave that for
others, I simply collect with notation--and I tire easily.

(There are some earlier works on this matter, not submitted for the
subject of approval.)

ALP, 8-17-96 [1] (Book 37)
