A professor of mine has said that Kierkegaard is not to be regarded as the "father of Christian Existentialism." He is an existentialist philosopher who happens to be a Christian. Martin Buber is considered to be an existentialist who is also Jewish. Sartre is an existentialist and is also atheist. (etc.)-- This is the trouble with labels.

Paul Tillich has said that Existentialism presents the question of human existence and Christianity is the answer. If this is true, then it is important for Christians to become familiar with the themes that gain clarity in this tradition. For that matter, all religions can speak to the heart of modern man only insofar as they can deal with existential problems (angst, guilt, and the like).

The same professor, mentioned above, also said that Sartre has a kind of theology, albeit an a-theology. Sartre's thought would be much more palettable to Christians if he were not so insistant on his atheism.
