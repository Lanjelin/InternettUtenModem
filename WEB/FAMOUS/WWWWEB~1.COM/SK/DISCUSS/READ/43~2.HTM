The Subject: Re: Abraham
At 21:37:10 on 08/13/96, Anthony L. Pecoraro (alp@loop.com) wrote:

At 00:12:03 PST on 08-13-96, Kenneth Friedman wrote:

>At 20:29:37 PST on 08-05-96, J. Climacus wrote:
>
>> I think viewing Abraham as a model of correct behavior is to attempt to recast his actions in an ethical framework. I think the point of Abraham's dilemma is that the demands of faith are incommensurate with a life in, as you say, the polis. As a professor of mine once said, there is little to extract from the Abraham/Isaac story except maybe: It is a terrible to be in the presence of the Living God. For me, the story of Abraham, to the extent that it can be made into any kind of sense, is that the demands of God and faith will always be outside the matrix of conceptual thought. Ethical categories simply don't apply.
>
>I believe the Abraham/Isaac story is one that might only be viewed as being the ultimate example of ethical behavior and the relativity of ethics. The term ³ethical behavior² in my mind is a misnomer, and should rather be termed ³cultural behavior,² as the mindset of the peer is the mindset of oneself.
>Abraham was and for all time will be an example of God¹s influence on the thoughts of man, and the willingness of man to choose his ethical philosophy based upon not his own thoughts, but upon others¹, in this case - God¹s.
>

The mindset of the peer is oft the mindset in ignorance
by will, which is unworthy of advenient light, let alone
faith.

Faith for the peer in ignorance is the habit of culture
and can rightly be termed "ethical behavior in a relative
sense." Who thinks so freely devoid of the culture's
mutation?

Yes, indeed, Abraham is an example of God's influence on
the thoughts of man...as babe seeks sustenance from it
parental nurturer.

Yet man choosing his ethical philosophy
(or habitual cultural behavior) upon his
thoughts in a state of ignorance or in a state
approaching enlightenment (Divine Mind: God)
is inherently a matter of choice in action
-- a choice of will -- in the degree to which
man the brute can fathom (or catch), willingly,
his divine, omnipotent heritage: he as one of
myriad sparks stemming from Father's flame.

ALP, 8/13/96 (Book 37) c.1996
