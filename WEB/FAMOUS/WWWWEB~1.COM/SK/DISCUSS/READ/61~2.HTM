The Subject: Re: SK on Objective Truth
At 21:50:41 on 08/18/96, J. Climacus (brettdel@mail.aros.net) wrote:

At 20:40:27 PST on 08-18-96, Steve wrote:

>I've just begun reading some of Kierkegaard's works this summer, and I've been wondering about his thoughts on objective truth. I'd appreciate anyone's thoughts on this:
>
>Although he certainly and undeniably emphasizes the place of subjective truth, that truth is subjectivity, I've gotten the impression that he believed very strongly in "objective truth," but that his circumstances dictated that his overwhelming emphasis in his writings being on the subjective side.
>
>In other words, he very strongly spoke of the response of an individual to God. But doesn't that imply that he believed that there was an "objective" God that we must subjectively respond to?
>
>Thanks for any thoughts on this.

My sense is that you are right, that Kierkegaard believed there was an objective truth, but that it was inaccessible from the finite perspectives of humanity. On the other hand, SK also seems to stress passionate beliefs as somehow providing a conduit to truth. By this, I don't think he means we can, if we believe hard enough and passionately enough, make 2 + 2 equal anything but four. But once we get beyond mere tautologies or perhaps brute facts such as the height of Mt. McKinley, there is a lot of wiggle room. Certainly, there is a lot of wiggle room when it comes to God. So, perhaps what Kierkegaard is saying is that the only truths worth getting worked up about are those that one MUST get worked up about. "Truth is an objective uncertainty held fast in an appropriation process of the most passionate inwardness." I think that's why he reacted so strongly against the institutionalized religion of his day. God was simply too important to simply be pocketed along with algorithms and other items of common sense. For a human being, this process is the most important avenue to the truth.
