The Subject: living in the universal
At 21:55:15 on 07/04/96, J.D. Rogers (j-rogers@sjca.edu) wrote:

In Fear and Trembling, SK makes what I think is an incredibly
persuasive and poetic argument regarding the nature of faith.
Employing Abraham as the main example, he suggests that
the essential act of faith is the transcendence of the ethical
or the universal--that is the regular world, the domain of
classical philosophy--and is the engagement of the absolute
with the absolute (God). He suggests that faith, given its
personal nature and relationship with the divine,
puts the single individual higher than the universal
(I read that as the polis, the place where ethics must reign supreme.
This analysis of faith while it seems true to me beckons the question
which I think is at the core of what we call "Christian ethics" today,
that is, how does this person of faith live in the world... in the
polis where ethics must be foremeost?
At the moment of faith for Abraham, in the context of the ethical,
he is a murderer. Can a person of faith such as Abraham even exist
in the world? Is Abraham's faith just an ideal which those of us in
the world are supposed to model after? If so, how can we attain it
while still living within the domain of the ethical? 

j.d.
