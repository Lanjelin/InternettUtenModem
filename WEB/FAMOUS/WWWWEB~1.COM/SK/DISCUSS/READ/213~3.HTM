The Subject: Re: The role of history in faith.
At 08:19:32 on 11/20/96, j. dooley (cortese@mail.utexas.edu) wrote:


>So, to say in a short paragraph what I've said in two pages, faith and truth and facts are all bound up together in a way that puts SK's Absolute, and the Chrstian God at risk of being overtaken by events. How and why do we protect faith from being eaten up by conflicting facts, and how do we protect ourselves from falling into a faith that is solipsistic: not communicable because not based on universals, not absolute because it is not a relation to anything but ourselves? Don't we all face, without anyone ever having an option to opt out, the dual risks of untrammeled subjectivity and bad faith (faith in what doesn't exist), and isn't the risk of getting the truth wrong a real problem raised by SK's leap of faith?

Most of the existentialism I have seen reeks of fetid solipsism.
Existentialism's daddy tells us We can't communicate, yet we must. (and he deosn't tell us in the form of imperative, nbut instead he tells us by example... indirectly) In the words of his metaphor,
SK tells us to widen, not to narrow, the gulf between us -- to believe, to delve into subjectivity; and then, yes, that danger is there, the danger that we drown in our own solipsism, and that danger appears more and more ominous the wider the chasm becomes; and there is where 'faith' comes in, 'faith' to leap out there, to jump across the chasm, to know that you will land on the other side, even if it is but a speck in the far off distance and your legs are weak and trembly -- hence, SK spends almost his entire life figuring out how to Communicate, to leap, get across that gap...
Does he suceed?
Was he happy?
