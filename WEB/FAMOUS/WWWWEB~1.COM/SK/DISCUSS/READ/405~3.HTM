At 19:11:40 on 03/08/97, Steve (SteveFisher111@msn.com) wrote:

At 12:53:55 PST on 01-06-97, L. Rempe wrote:

>I need two pieces of information. Where did Kierkegaard stand on the subject of the Holy Trinity? On the Abraham/Isaac sacrifice story? Is there a good ten cent explanation? If not, can you direct me to any of his writings?

As for the subject of the trinity, I have very little reason to believe that he doubted the doctrine in any way. He was in all respects a very (theologically) orthodox Lutheran. Further, I've found that most people who deny the doctrine of the trinity do so as a logical necessity to their denial of the deity of the person of Christ. As to the second person of the trinity, SK was vehement on his affirmation of Jesus being fully God and fully man, for a discussion of this see somewhere around the middle of "Practice in Christianity" (I loaned the book to someone, I can't look it up myself.) But I remember his discussion on the "offense" of the God-man must be overcome by embracing him both as a simple man who was just like the rest of us, at the same time as embracing him as the eternal God who created the universe. This doctrine is so closely linked to the trinity, I would think he believed just as strongly in the trinity as in Jesus divinity.

But I don't have any better answer off the top of my head. Hope this helps somewhat.
