December 20, 1996

Spielberg up for Corwin award

From The Hollywood Reporter:

Steven Spielberg will be honored next month with the American Jewish Committee's Sherrill C. Corwin Human Relations Award. The award will be presented to the director Jan. 23 during a dinner at the Regent Beverly Wilshire Hotel. Oprah Winfrey, who appeared in Spielberg's "The Color Purple," will be the keynote speaker, and Spielberg's DreamWorks SKG partners David Geffen and Jeffrey Katzenberg will serve as honorary chairs with Edgar Bronfman Jr., Clint Eastwood, Lucy Fisher, George Lucas, Sid Sheinberg and Bruce Ramer. The Corwin award recognizes individuals in the entertainment industry who have promoted humanitarian causes. In addition to Sheinberg and Eastwood, previous winners include Jack Valenti, Bob Hope, Frank Mancuso, Ted Turner, Bob Iger, Jeff Sagansky and Lucie Salhany.

December 18, 1996

Spielberg Was Anonymous Buyer Of Gable's Oscar

From Reuters/Variety:
By David Finnigan

BEVERLY HILLS, Calif. (Reuter) - Filmmaker Steven Spielberg anonymously purchased Clark Gable's 1934 Oscar for a record $550,000 at a weekend auction, then donated it Tuesday to the Academy of Motion Picture Arts and Sciences.

The academy, which hands out Hollywood's annual Oscars, had fought unsuccessfully to bar Sunday's auction of the 12-inch gold-plated statuette that Gable won for his role in the movie ''It Happened One Night.''

``Bless Steven Spielberg,'' an emotional academy president Arthur Hiller told reporters. He said officials of the academy, which had argued the Oscars were never meant as articles of trade, had no advance knowledge of Spielberg's plans and were ''still reeling from the breathtaking generosity of Steven's action.''

``The man has a hell of a way with a holiday gift,'' Hiller added.

In a letter to the academy after the auction, the Oscar-winning director wrote: ``If not with the Clark Gable estate, I could think of no better sanctuary for Gable's only Oscar than the motion picture academy.

``The Oscar statuette is the most personal recognition of good work our industry can ever bestow, and it strikes me as a sad sign of our times that this icon could be confused with a commercial treasure,'' he said.

Spielberg was also the anonymous bidder who bought Gable's personal script for the 1939 film classic ``Gone With the Wind'' for $220,000 Sunday at Christie's Los Angeles auction house. But there were no plans for Spielberg to donate the script to the academy along with the Oscar, academy officials said. Both were sold by Gable's estate.

Spielberg, the world's most commercially successful filmmaker who directed such blockbusters as ``E.T.'' and ''Jurassic Park,'' won Oscars for best picture and best director in 1994 for his Holocaust epic ``Schindler's List.''

Heaping praise on Spielberg, Hiller said: ``Steven not only epitomizes filmmaking, he really epitomizes caring. Lots of people care, but Steven does something about it.''

The identity of the buyer of Gable's Oscar was shrouded in mystery Sunday when the winning bid was made by telephone during an auction of Hollwyood artifacts and memorabilia.

The bid of $550,000 topped the previous record price for an Oscar of $510,000, set in 1993 at Sotheby's auction house for the statuette given to Vivien Leigh when she was named best actress for her role opposite Gable in ``Gone With the Wind.''

Hiller said academy officials were contacted Sunday by Spielberg's office and were told that he wanted to donate the Oscar to the academy. It was turned over Tuesday, and Hiller said it would be placed on permanent display at the academy's Center for Motion Picture Study in Beverly Hills.

Last Friday, the academy had asked a Los Angeles judge to block the sale ofGable's Oscar. But the judge refused and the auction went ahead as planned.

Including auction fees, which are paid by the buyer, the Oscar was bought for $607,500, and the script went for a total of $244,500.

Gable, who died in 1960 at the age of 59, won the Oscar for his role as a wisecracking newspaper reporter in love with a runaway heiress, played by Claudette Colbert. ``It Happened One Night'' swept the Academy Awards, with Colbert, director Frank Capra and the film itself also being honored.

November 27, 1996

Spielberg, Lucas, and other directors still oppose TV pact

From The Hollywood Reporter:

A group of filmmakers -- including Steven Spielberg, George Lucas and Bernardo Bertolucci -- wrote to FCC commissioner Susan Ness Tuesday, stating opposition to a Monday compromise over proposed digital TV standards that may favor a 16:9 aspect ratio over alternate 2:1 aspect ratio. "With the likelihood of wider TV screens in the digital future, we are dismayed at the news that the FCC has indicated that it may approve conclusions reached by negotiations between computer industries, television broadcasters and television set manufacturers that do not provide for redress of the grievance (regarding) transmission in the digital world of the original aspect ratio of the motion pictures we make," stated the letter by the Coalition of Film Makers. Filmmakers fear most digital-TV broadcasts of their movies will continue the historic alteration of theatrical-release images to fit on TV screens of different proportions. (Carl DiOrio)

November 7, 1996

'Amistad' Spielberg's first DreamWorks directing project


From the Reuters/Variety Entertainment Summary:

By Ted Johnson

HOLLYWOOD (Variety) - Steven Spielberg plans to follow his sequel to ``Jurassic Park'' with a movie about mutinous slaves, a project that will mark his first directing project for the DreamWorks partnership he co-founded last year.

``Amistad'' is the true story of a mutiny on a slave ship of the same name in 1839, centering on Cinque, an African captive who led the rebellion. When the captives' attempt to sail home failed, they were captured off the Atlantic coast.

The story follows their struggle in the U.S. as they fight their case in the court. Former president John Quincy Adams defends them and takes their battle to the Supreme Court.

``Steven was taken from the outset with the possibilities of telling an intimate story between two extraordinary men -- an African slave and an ex-president of the United States -- set against an epic historical backdrop,'' the film's executive producer Walter Parkes said in a statement.

Also known as ``Black Mutiny,'' ``Amistad'' is written by David Franzoni (Citizen Cohn) and Steve Zaillian (Schindler's List), who is doing a rewrite.

Production is expected to begin in February, with filming taking place in Los Angeles, New England and the Caribbean. Spielberg is currently filming ``The Lost World,'' the sequel to ''Jurassic Park.''

After ``Amistad,'' he will direct Tom Hanks in ``Saving Private Ryan.'' Hanks will play a soldier who is forced to undertake an unusual mission to save a private trapped behind enemy lines during the Allied invasion of Europe.

October 16, 1996

New Zealand Gears Up To Host 'Jurassic' Sequel

From the Reuters/Variety Entertainment Summary:

By Victoria Batchelor

WELLINGTON (Reuter) - A rugged wilderness area of New Zealand's South Island will provide the scenic backdrop to Steven Spielberg's ``Jurassic Park'' sequel.

``Certainly, on a film of this kind, we had the option of shooting just about anywhere and, after looking at a variety of sites, the area in New Zealand was deemed to be absolutely the best,'' film publicist Don Levy said on Wednesday.

New Zealand's Department of Conservation on Tuesday approved an application from Spielberg's company Amblin Entertainment to film in Fiordland, one of the world's biggest national parks, for the movie: ``The Lost World: Jurassic Park.''

The original 1993 dinosaur blockbuster, which was filmed on the Hawaiian island of Kauai, was the highest-grossing film of all time, earning US$913 million in worldwide box office receipts.

Fiordland, a vast area of 1.2 million hectares (2.96 million acres) with a population of barely 3,000, will offer a stunning backdrop of rainforest, lakes, granite peaks and waterfalls hundreds of metres high.

``We scouted locations quite literally around the world and are very pleased to have settled the location in New Zealand,'' Levy said, adding the final decision was Spielberg's.

``We will shoot scenes that typically establish a sense of place. It doesn't involve actors, but it expands the look of the film.''

The film crew will shoot for five days in December, based in Te Anau, the main town on the edge of the park.

The ``Lost World'' sequel, starring Jeff Goldblum, Julianne Moore and Sir Richard Attenborough, takes place on an island six years after the events depicted in Jurassic Park.

``Amblin first came here in January this year, so we've been dealing with them for 10 months, but what has been impressive to us has been their previous experience filming in national parks in the United States and Australia,'' said Conservation Department Southland regional conservator Lou Sanson.

He described the area as ``very much dinosaur country.'' However, velociraptors and brontosauruses will not be taking over New Zealand just yet.

``Essentially the idea is to film the dinosaurs in Hollywood and splice them into the scenery,'' Sanson said. ``But we understand Spielberg will be here.''

He said the crew was paying a standard fee for the privilege of filming in the area. ``It's nothing different just because it's Steven Spielberg.''

Locals are excited at the prospect of Hollywood coming to their doorsteps. Money spent on accommodation, flights and food will boost the area's economy, said Jan Wilson, chief executive of Fiordland Promotions.

``It's a highlight for the area to have such a prestigious movie being filmed here,'' she told Reuters.

``But we'll take it in our stride. We had (British pop star) Cliff Richard here for a week in 1990/91. We're quite excited, but life goes on.''

October 7, 1996

Absentee Steven Spielberg lets writer do the helming

From the Reuters/Variety Entertainment Summary:

Technological advancements in fiber optics made it possible for Steven Spielberg to travel to Poland to direct his Oscar-winning ``Schindler's List'' while simultaneously supervising the post-production on ``Jurassic Park.''

Now, as he directs the ``Jurassic'' sequel, ``The Lost World'', technology again has come to his aid. When he was called away to the Hamptons for several days, Spielberg didn't have to shut down production. Instead, he had a monitor trained on the action, letting his screenwriter, David Koepp, essentially be the first-unit director. A similar monitor was set up in the Hamptons so Koepp could see him.

A spokesman says Spielberg gave cues to Koepp, who recently made his directing debut with ``The Trigger Effect'', and got the shots he needed without having to be on the premises. The spokesman says it was just a few days and that Spielberg is now back, directing in person once again. The film bows next Memorial Day.

September 21, 1996

Spielberg named to USC Board of Trustees

From the USC Daily Trojan:

Legendary filmmaker Steven Spielberg, who has played a vital role in the School of Cinema-Television, has been elected to the USC Board of Trustees.

"I am very enthusiastic about the appointment," President Steven B. Sample said. "The performing arts are a very strong department (at USC), probably the strongest of any university in the country. Mr. Spielberg is the first practicing artist to be appointed to the board and his experience and notable success will be a great addition to the board."

"USC is widely respected for its cinema school, and it is an anchor institution in the overall communications program," Sample said. "The board is trying to recruit more people with strong ties to the arts, and Mr. Spielberg was a natural choice."

Spielberg received an honorary doctorate of fine arts from USC in 1994. He currently serves as a member of the School of Cinema-Television's Board of Councilors, and has donated time, money, and leadership to the cinema school. He funded the Steven Spielberg Music Scoring Stage, which was dedicated in 1984.

"Members of the board have legal ownership of the university...on behalf on students, alumni and future studnets," Sample said.

Spielberg has directed or produced five of the 20 top-grossing films of all time, including "ET: The Extraterrestrial," "Jurassic Park" and "The Color Purple." His most recent film was the internationally acclaimed "Schindler's List," which received seven Oscars, including Best Picture and Best Director, at the 1993 Academy Awards.

In 1994, he founded the new studio DreamWorks SKG with Jeffrey Katzenberg and David Geffen. He is currently directing "The Lost World: Jurassic Park II" for release next summer.

"As a member (of the board), Spielberg will help approve major policies, choose officers, and set up chief standards for the university," Sample said. "We are delighted to have such a renowned artist with us."

By Erin Phibbs (Staff Writer)

August 7, 1996

New Info on DreamWorks Studio

From the September issue of Cinescape:

"MR. SPIELBERG BUILDS HIS DREAM HOME

Steven Spielberg recently unveiled several features of his latest playground, the new DreamWorks studio complex. Located in Playa Vista outside Los Angeles, the new moviemaking facility is the first studio to be designed with significant input from a director, and it incorporates some unique features that were added to Spielberg's specifications. The new soundstages will have built-in, fully automated lighting grids, and the studio's production offices will have a view of the films being shot on the stages.

Playa Vista will also boast a tank custom-designed for underwater filming, "something all directors are crying for," says Spielberg, and the stages will also have removable skylights that will allow sets to be lit by natural sunshine.

Construction on the new complex is scheduled to begin before the end of this year."
