December 18, 1996

DreamWorks studio may be back on track

From The Hollywood Reporter:

A DreamWorks SKG official said that things appear to be back on track at Playa Vista, the troubled real estate development that is to serve as the studio's first permanent home. DreamWorks has been vocal in recent months about its belief that Maguire Thomas was allowing the Playa Vista project to fall apart by inflexibility on certain financial issues. But Montgomery said a recent move to give former Maguire Thomas executive James Thomas control of the project instead of Maguire Thomas head Rob Thomas has spurred progress on securing $150 million in first-phase financing. DreamWorks recently held high-level talks with officials of Westinghouse Electric Corp. about selling its CBS facilities in Studio City, which CBS nixed, although a source close to the talks suggests it might be possible to negotiate a partial sale of the facility to DreamWorks, with Westinghouse holding a continuing stake. (Carl DiOrio)

December 4, 1996

Environmentalists file lawsuit against DreamWorks project

From Reuters/Variety:

LOS ANGELES (Reuter) - A group of environmentalists filed a federal lawsuit Tuesday seeking to block the planned construction of DreamWorks SKG's studio development project at a site near the Playa del Rey beachfront section of Los Angeles.

The suit, filed against in U.S. Federal Court in Los Angeles against the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers, claimed the agency violated terms of the Clean Water Act and other federal laws when it approved the developers' provisional permit to fill, dredge or pave over about 28 wetlands acres of wetlands to build. DreamWorks was not named in the lawsuit.

The lawsuit covers a small portion of the 1,000 acres set aside for the Playa Vista project, the name of the development that is expected to house the DreamWorks headquarters as well as other corporate offices.

The site is situated in the Ballona wetlands north of Los Angeles International Airport.

The planned DreamWorks studio -- owned by Steven Spielberg, former Walt Disney Co. executive Jeffrey Katzenberg and music mogul David Geffen -- is not part of the acreage.

But the land will include storm and sewage run-off from DreamWorks, making the project dependant on it.

The Playa Vista development had been planned for years, but gained strength in 1995 when the DreamWorks trio, looking to build a studio, signed on.

The Los Angeles City Council gave the project $70 million in tax breaks, outraging environmentalists who claimed the city was subsidizing millionaires.

The lawsuit is part of a continuing legal battle between environmentalists seeking to preserve all of the wetlands and developers who say they can build and still preserve part of wetlands.

Earlier this year, environmentalists lost a state court appeal to stop Playa Vista, and in October filed an appeal.

The status of the $8 billion project has become questionable following friction between the developer Maguire Thomas Partners and potential tenants, including DreamWorks.

Already one tenant, International Business Machines Corp. has decided not to participate in the project.

A spokeswoman for Maguire Thomas said the developer was confident the court would rule in its favor and construction would begin in the first half of next year.

DreamWorks declined to comment.

DreamWorks over Scandinavia

From the Hollywood Reporter:

DreamWorks, SKG has inked a long-term exclusive output deal with Scandinavian Broadcasting System, one of Europe's premiere broadcasting companies. November 15, 1996

Stan Winston signs deal with DreamWorks

From the Reuters/Variety Entertainment Summary:

By Anita M. Busch

HOLLYWOOD (Variety) - Special effects wizard Stan Winston, who is working on Steven Spielberg's Jurassic Park sequel The Lost World, has signed a filmmaking deal with DreamWorks.

In addition to producing and directing feature films, he is expected to get involved in other areas of entertainment at the studio such as the possible development of an animatronic theme park attraction.

Winston said he has developed a handful of projects over the years and has about half a dozen under his new company. There is only one set up at a studio, an untitled horror project at Disney's Touchstone banner.

The special-effects guru has a close association with DreamWorks principal Spielberg through his work on Jurassic Park and Lost World, for which he is supervising the design and creation of the live-action dinosaurs. He also worked with another DreamWorks principal, David Geffen, on Interview With the Vampire.

Winston has won special effects Oscars for Jurassic Park, Terminator 2: Judgment Day and Aliens.

``There are about three or four projects in development here that could be naturals for Stan to get involved with, but we're also looking for him to come up with his own ideas,'' said Walter Parkes, president of feature development for DreamWorks.

Winston said he will now actively start seeking projects to acquire. He noted, ``Fortunately, DreamWorks is a young major so I'm one of a handful and won't get lost in a slew of other production deals.

``Steven and I have also talked about our mutual love of theme park attractions,'' Winston added. ``I always wanted to create the ultimate animatronic theme park attraction. We have so many similar interests.''

November 15, 1996

CBS says NO to DreamWorks on Studio City

From the Hollywood Reporter:

CBS Inc. on Thursday issued a terse denial that it is considering the sale of its landmark production facility on Radford Avenue in Studio City to DreamWorks SKG. DreamWorks officials refused to confirm or deny that talks are under way to take over the facility and declined comment on the CBS statement. However, a well-placed DreamWorks insider continued to insist that talks have been held in an effort to persuade CBS parent Westinghouse Electric Corp. to sell the Studio City lot. The Studio City site would serve as an alternative to DreamWorks' taking space in the troubled Playa Vista commercial-residential development to be built near Playa del Rey. Other than some site clearing, work has yet to begin and a planned 1998 completion for Playa Vista now is considered impossible. "CBS is always interested in creative business opportunities," the network said. "But there is no scenario under which we would sell or give up control of (CBS) Studio Center." (Carl DiOrio)

From the Reuters/Variety Entertainment Summary:

By Ted Johnson

HOLLYWOOD (Variety) - CBS officials Thursday denied speculation the company would sell its production facility in Studio City to DreamWorks, which is having problems with its planned site in Playa Vista.

Studio Center, the 66-year-old former Republic Pictures lot where John Wayne began his career, had been mentioned in news reports as an alternative to the Playa Vista development near Los Angeles International Airport.

The fledgling DreamWorks and the lead developer of Playa Vista, Maguire Thomas Partners, have been deadlocked over securing additional investors to get the project moving.

DreamWorks produces Ink on the Studio City lot in the San Fernando Valley, and has held talks about the leasing of additional soundstage space; however, there have been doubts as to how viable it would be as an alternative to the kind of studio DreamWorks envisions in Playa Vista, where more space is available.

The CBS lot is already crowded with production of shows such as Roseanne and Seinfeld.

``CBS is always interested in creative business opportunities,'' a CBS statement said. ``But there is no scenario under which we would sell or give up control of Studio Center.''

DreamWorks officials said Maguire has been unwilling to cede control of the Playa Vista project as a way to attract a new investor. Sources said the project needs about $150 million in equity to start its first phase.

Over the past few weeks, there has been an increasing amount of pressure placed on Maguire to take a new role or accept an offer in which he would have a smaller equity stake. IBM sent a letter to him announcing that it would not take part in the project as its technology architect. It also demanded more than $400,000 in consulting fees.

November 6, 1996

Delays dog DreamWorks Studios

From the Reuters/Variety Entertainment Summary:

By Ted Johnson

HOLLYWOOD (Variety) - A bold plan by the fledgling DreamWorks studio to convert a coastal Los Angeles suburb into a vast business and housing development is encountering obstacles as partners and potential tenants grow restless.

DreamWorks had announced to much fanfare in December that it would locate its new studio at a former Hughes Aircraft facility in Playa Vista, just north of Los Angeles International Airport. IBM, special effects house Digital Domain and Silicon Graphics expressed strong interest in moving in as well.

And at the same time, studio founders Steven Spielberg, Jeffrey Katzenberg and David Geffen became partners in all of Playa Vista -- a massive 1,087-acre development of homes, offices and the studio -- along with property developers Maguire Thomas and the Rouse Corp.

But a groundbreaking originally scheduled for last June now looks at least six months away. And there is uncertainty about a lawsuit challenging the City of Los Angeles' approval of the studio. Although developers emerged victorious last summer, the ruling has been appealed.

In the meantime, IBM has decided to not take space at the site, and speculation has surfaced that other tenants are also set to pass on the project, highly placed sources familiar with the deal said Tuesday.

Digital Domain is now eyeing a site not far from the company's offices in Venice, according to the same sources.

Additionally, lead developer Maguire Thomas Partners, DreamWorks and other partners are struggling with issues including pricing, control of the project and how financing will be structured. Among the problems: overcoming existing debt on the property incurred after the real estate market fell flat in the early 1990s, before DreamWorks was involved.

The delays in the project have come as a surprise to City Hall officials and others in the entertainment business as well as to DreamWorks.

Developers also point out that the project is complex, and original plans to break ground in June probably were too optimistic. Plans call for creating no less than a community of the future, connecting homes, offices and the studio with fiber optics, and at the same time, building the first major studio in 70 years.

But highly placed sources said DreamWorks has been frustrated by issues concerning who would control the project as it seeks ways to come up with $150 million to $200 million needed to launch the development.

Sources close to the project have groused that Robert Maguire has stood firm on staying in the lead position, even though it is likely he would have to step back should a new investor take a major stake.

Still, others have suggested that, with negotiations between the real estate company and the partners becoming tense, DreamWorks and its partners may be posturing to put pressure on Maguire Thomas.

A Maguire Thomas spokeswoman rejected the notion that Maguire would hold up the project because of control issues. The top priority, she said, is coming up with a financing structure, which she pointed out would be complex no matter who was in charge.

Meanwhile, tenants such as Digital Domain have had concerns not only about lease rates on the property, but when the space would be available. With groundbreaking uncertain, DreamWorks has inquired about space at CBS Studio Center in Studio City as a contingency plan, sources said.

Digital Domain CEO Scott Ross said he hasn't made any decisions about leasing space, though he acknowledged that the company's commercial division has grown and needs to move by Jan. 1.

Also getting antsy about the project are city officials, who are in the final stages on a $70 million incentive package designed to lure DreamWorks to the site. But the incentives voted on by the City Council hinge on the presence of DreamWorks in the project.

October 21, 1996

DreamWorks sitcom Ink review (premieres tonight)

From the Reuters/Variety Entertainment Summary:

Ink (Mon. (21), 8:30-9 p.m., CBS) By Jeremy Gerard

HOLLYWOOD (Variety) - Faced with a pilot and several episodes that were generating nuclear-disaster-type buzz, DreamWorks' Jeffrey Katzenberg and CBS' Les Moonves recruited Murphy Browncreator Diane English to rebuild ``Ink'' from the bottom up -- and gave her all of eight weeks to do it.

On the line were the reputations of Ted Danson, the Emmy-winning star of the long-running ``Cheers,'' and his Oscar-winning wife, Mary Steenburgen; several million dollars already spent on developing a sitcom for them; and CBS' hopes for a primetime home run.

CBS didn't get a very good sitcom out of that pressure cooker, but it did get a home run.

``Ink'' has a long way to go in working out a style and gaining some consistency of tone. Moreover, its setting, the New York Sun, bears about as much resemblance to an actual city newspaper as Murphy Brown's ``F.Y.I.'' bears to a real TV newsmagazine.

But people will no more tune into ``Ink'' for a journalism seminar than they do ``Murphy Brown'' for a glimpse behind the camera at ``60 Minutes.''

They tune in for the stars, and the stars of ``Ink'' -- individually and in tandem -- deliver.

They're great fun to spend 23 minutes with. Danson plays Mike Logan, the Sun's star metro columnist. His face appears on the sides of buses; people give him the thumbs up in the street; vendors have breakfast waiting to press into his hands as he flies by on the way to work. Mike owns the city.

The mayor's furious about this morning's column? Too bad. The borough of Queens is suing? Tra-la. What really wrecks the day is the appearance of Kate Montgomery (Steenburgen), Mike's globe-trotting journalist ex-wife, who is globe-trotting no more.

She's the Sun's new managing editor, hence Mike's boss, having arrived with their teenage daughter, Abby (Alana Austin), in tow.

The Sun fields an exceptionally high-spirited, nice, how shall I say, chipper staff, in quarters so unnaturally clean that it's impossible to imagine anyone coming into contact with the substance that gives the show its name.

The cast of characters is, at this point, painfully predictable: a neurotic (read: Jewish) financial reporter (Saul Rubinek), a police reporter (Ernie Trainor) who's sqeamish about blood stains, a gung-ho editorial assistant (Donna French).

And, of course, a run-down, long-in-the-tooth society columnist (Christine Ebersole) given to pronouncements along the lines of, ``I've got a hangover that would take down Kitty Dukakis.''

Not all of ``Ink'' is that tasteless, but a lot of it is that stupid.

Much is made of Mike's horror over Abby's confession that she's a Republican. The ``Metro-bomber'' calls Kate, only to be met with a harangue about the trials of finding an apartment in the city and quitting smoking. You get the picture.

And yet Danson and Steenburgen are delightful. He is the master of preening self-love and she, even outfitted in hideous clothes, appears completely at ease before the camera.

This clearly owes something to the presence of veteran sitcom director Thomas Schlamme at the helm; for all their ragged plot edges, the scenes flow effortlessly.

By the end of the first episode, the only thing established worth keeping is that Mike and Kate face a real challenge working together, and that it will be amusing to watch them figure out how.

Everything else is up for grabs.
